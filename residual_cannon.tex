\begin{abstract} % 253 words, 1909 characters
What if we could model 100\,\% of a stellar spectrum? Modern surveys infer stellar properties by comparing observed spectra with synthetic models, yet even the best fits leave structured residuals. We analyse 7087 GALAH spectra and ask what information remains after subtracting each best-fitting model. Across 99 million residual pixels, 4.6 million exceed $3\sigma$, far above the 0.3 million expected from Gaussian noise. We map the amplitude, significance, sign, and recurrence of these residuals and identify 380 significant regions. Comparison with stellar atlases, line lists, diffuse interstellar bands, and sky diagnostics links most regions to plausible causes: 63\,\% are candidate line-strength or line-profile mismatches, while others trace missing or unidentified lines, interstellar absorption, sky emission, or broader reduction structure. We then train data-driven models on the residual spectra themselves. Residual-only models recover $T_\mathrm{eff}$ to $80-150\,\mathrm{K}$ and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ to $0.059-0.086\,\mathrm{dex}$, depending on sample quality, and retain weaker, partly indirect information about elemental abundances. Extinction-related coefficients recover the strongest known interstellar features, whereas chromospheric activity leaves plausible Balmer-line signatures but is not recovered robustly out of sample. Most strikingly, the 6\,\% of pixels excluded from the GALAH optimisation recover stellar labels almost as well as the full residual spectrum and better than the remaining 94\,\% alone. Residuals are therefore not merely imperfections to discard: they map missing line data, incomplete physics, unfitted astrophysical components, and observational systematics. Reading between the lines turns one spectroscopist's trash into another one's treasure, and provides a practical route toward extracting more of the information already present in stellar spectra.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

% Big picture: spectra encode stellar, Galactic, and planetary information.
Stellar spectra are among the most information-rich observables in astrophysics. Their absorption and emission features encode stellar atmospheric parameters, elemental abundances, line-formation physics, stellar activity, and non-stellar absorption along the line of sight. Large spectroscopic surveys use this information to reconstruct the chemical and dynamical history of galaxies, to trace the production and recycling of elements, and to connect stellar populations to the formation history of our Milky Way \citep[e.g.][]{FreemanBlandHawthorn2002, BlandHawthorn_Gerhard2016} and other galaxies \citep{VandeVen2025}. High-precision stellar spectra are also central to exoplanet science, where planetary radial-velocity signals have to be disentangled from stellar variability, photospheric line-profile changes, telluric contamination, and instrumental systematics \citep[e.g.][]{Fischer2016}. The scientific return from stellar spectra is therefore not set only by the number of observed stars or stellar populations, but also by how completely we can extract the information encoded in each observed photon.

% Hook: what if we could fit every pixel?
This raises a deliberately provocative question: what if we could fit 100\% of a stellar spectrum? In practice, every stellar spectroscopic analysis leaves residuals between the observed spectrum and the best-fitting model. These residuals are often treated as a quality-control diagnostic or as a nuisance to be masked, but they may also encode astrophysical, observational, and modelling information that the adopted spectral model has not captured. This is not only a conceptual issue: in the Galactic Archaeology with HERMES (GALAH) survey, one of the large-scale stellar spectroscopic surveys of the Milky Way, machine-learning interpolation made it computationally feasible to increase the fraction of the spectrum used for stellar-parameter and abundance fitting from typically $\sim20\%$ in Data Release 3 \citep[DR3,][]{Buder2021} to $87\%$ in Data Release 4 \citep[DR4,][]{Buder2025b}. However, even this modern synthesis could only use up to $94\%$ of the spectrum outside the typically neglected telluric O$_2$ Fraunhofer A-band region, because some observed spectral regions still could not be described reliably by the model \citep[][Sec.~4.3.2]{Buder2025b}. The central premise of this work is that the residual spectrum itself is a useful scientific object: it records where and how the model differs from the photons.

% State of the field: physical and data-driven models.
The modelling of stellar spectra has advanced substantially over the last decade \citep{Nissen2018, Jofre2019}. Modern survey pipelines \citep[see Tab.~1 of][]{Ness2026b} now increasingly infer many stellar labels simultaneously and across broad wavelength ranges, either through direct comparison to synthetic spectra or through flexible data-driven and emulator-based approaches \citep[e.g.][]{Ness2015, Casey2016, Ting2019}. Data-driven methods now play several roles in spectroscopy, including spectral generation, label inference, calibration, component separation, denoising, and residual analysis \citep[see][for a review]{Ness2026b}.

% Gap: models remain incomplete.
Despite this progress, no current survey analysis reproduces every observed pixel at the level implied by the observational uncertainties. Residuals can arise from several broad classes of effects: incomplete input physics, such as uncertain atomic and molecular data, missing blends, or incomplete hyperfine-structure treatment \citep{Heiter2021}; analysis choices, such as imperfect continuum normalisation \citep{Jofre2017}; fixed abundance-pattern assumptions, such as adopting solar-scaled abundance mixtures or fixed isotopic ratios despite their effect on line profiles \citep[e.g.][]{Coelho2007, Yong2004, Masseron2021}; missing stellar physics, such as limitations of 1-dimensional LTE or approximate non-LTE line formation, chromospheric activity, and spots \citep{Amarsi2018, Masseron2021, Lind2024, Lisogorskyi2019, Spina2020, Wilson2023b, Nordlander2024, Bergemann2026}; and non-stellar contaminants, such as interstellar absorption, telluric absorption, and sky subtraction residuals \citep{Saydjari2023, Casey2026}. This list is far from complete and does not even include one of the major unresolved complications in stellar spectroscopy: unresolved stellar multiplicity, where residuals from a single-star model can be further amplified by the unmodelled spectral contribution of one or more unresolved companions \citep{ElBadry2018, Merle2024}, which we acknowledge but bracket out of the present single-star residual audit. Regardless of their origin, positive and negative residuals provide more than a measure of fit quality: they provide an empirical map of the information that remains after the adopted model has been applied. An insightful example is the exploration of molecular C2 and CN features in the GALAH wavelength range. Originally not expected to be measureable as part of GALAH \citep{DeSilva2015}, probes of strongly carbon-enhanced stars \citep{Cotar2019b} proved fruitful for the rollout of C and N measurements for a significant portion of stars with less strong C2 and CN features \citep{Buder2025b}.

% From residuals as diagnostics to residuals as models.
Data-driven spectral models provide a natural way to test whether residuals contain structured information. \textsc{The Cannon} \citep{Ness2015, Casey2016}, as one of the most widely used data-driven fitting approaches, learns the dependence of continuum-normalised flux on stellar labels directly from a reference set, and its application to high-dimensional abundance space has shown that learned spectral derivatives can recover known line sensitivities and identify previously unrecognised abundance-sensitive features \citep[e.g.][]{Buder2018}. More broadly, \citet{Ness2026b} emphasised that residual analyses can uncover non-stellar signals and open a discovery space, while also stressing that data-driven models do not replace theoretical models. In this work, we apply this logic to residual spectra rather than to the observed spectra themselves. The goal is not to replace the physical synthesis, but to ask which label-dependent information remains after the synthesis has already been subtracted.

% Prior work and scope: residuals have been used before, but here for a stellar-rest-frame audit.
Residual and component-separation approaches have already proved powerful for non-stellar spectral structure. Data-driven methods have recovered extinction and diffuse interstellar band information from large stellar spectroscopic data sets, including in LAMOST and GALAH spectra \citep[e.g.][]{Ho2017, Buder2018, Vogrincic2023}. Recent component-separation models, such as \textsc{MADGICS}, explicitly separate stellar, dust, sky, instrumental, and residual components in large spectroscopic data sets \citep{Saydjari2023}. These studies demonstrate that residuals can contain astrophysically useful information from several distinct sources. Here, we deliberately take a complementary approach: we keep the residuals in the stellar rest frame and ask what a stellar-spectroscopy residual audit reveals about fitted stellar labels, abundance patterns, extinction, activity, and model limitations.

% Why GALAH and why this sample?
GALAH is particularly well suited to such an audit because it provides observed spectra, best-fitting synthetic spectra, flux uncertainties, stellar parameters, elemental abundances, extinction estimates, quality information, and residual-based diagnostic products for a large, homogeneous optical data set \citep{DeSilva2015, Buder2025b}. We focus first on a representative three-night GALAH sample. This keeps the analysis compact and transparent while still sampling ordinary survey targets across the broad GALAH magnitude range and 2-degree field of view \citep{Martell2017}, rather than selecting stars a posteriori by stellar labels or residual behaviour. A full survey-wide residual model is left to future work. We also use external activity indicators to test whether residuals encode spectral variability that is not part of the single-star photospheric synthesis.

% Paper structure and scientific questions.
We approach the residual spectrum in two steps with the data that we will introduce in Sec.~\ref{sec:data}. First, we perform a systematic audit of the residuals (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_audit}): we quantify their significance, sign, wavelength localisation, and recurrence across the GALAH wavelength range, and compare the strongest residual features to plausible stellar, interstellar, telluric, sky, instrumental, and line-list origins. Second (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon}), we use a data-driven model to test how much information can be extracted from the residual spectra alone. This separates the question of where the synthesis and the observations disagree from the question of what information those disagreements retain.

% Four questions that structure the paper.
Under the broader question of what information remains in stellar-spectrum residuals, this work addresses two connected themes: the nature of the residual structure and the information encoded within it. These are developed through four specific questions, discussed further in Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,labelwidth=0em]
    \item How significant, systematic, and recurrent are the remaining residuals relative to the observational uncertainties, and how do they separate into features that are over-predicted or under-predicted by the synthesis?
    \item Which residual features can be associated with plausible stellar, interstellar, sky, instrumental, reduction, or line-data origins, and which of these provide actionable targets for improving the spectral modelling?
    \item To what extent do residual spectra retain information about properties already included in the synthesis fit, such as stellar parameters and elemental abundance patterns, as well as properties not explicitly fitted, such as interstellar extinction and chromospheric activity?
    \item Where within the spectrum is this information concentrated, and in particular, how much remains in wavelength regions excluded from the original GALAH DR4 optimisation?
\end{enumerate}

These questions frame residual modelling as a diagnostic rather than a replacement for physical synthesis. The aim is to determine which shortcomings can be addressed through improved line data or line profiles, additional stellar or nuisance labels, more realistic physical modelling, improved reduction procedures, or complementary data-driven corrections.

\section{Data} \label{sec:data}

% Data overview.
This work requires three linked ingredients for each spectrum: the observed continuum-normalised flux, the corresponding best-fitting synthetic flux, and reference labels or diagnostics against which residual structure can be tested. Section~\ref{sec:data_galah} describes the GALAH DR4 data products used in this work, including observed spectra and uncertainties, best-fitting synthetic spectra, stellar parameters, elemental abundances, extinction estimates, and residual-based diagnostic quantities. Section~\ref{sec:data_samples} then defines the parent three-night GALAH sample and the derived baseline, abundance, and activity samples used for the residual audit and data-driven residual modelling.

\subsection{GALAH DR4 spectra and labels}
\label{sec:data_galah}

We use data products from GALAH DR4 \citep{Buder2025b}, based on spectra observed with the HERMES spectrograph \citep{Barden2010, Brzeski2011, Heijmans2012, Farrell2014, Sheinis2015} on the Anglo-Australian Telescope as part of the GALAH survey \citep{DeSilva2015}. HERMES records high-resolution optical spectra with a nominal resolution of 28000 in four non-contiguous wavelength channels, covering the blue ($4713$--$4903\,\text{\AA}$), green ($5648$--$5873\,\text{\AA}$), red ($6478$--$6737\,\text{\AA}$), and near-infrared ($7585$--$7887\,\text{\AA}$) arms for up to 400 fibres of the 2dF position system \citep{Lewis2002, Miszalski2006}. GALAH DR4 combines observations from the main GALAH survey with K2-HERMES, TESS-HERMES, cluster, halo, and other targeted HERMES programs. We return to the specific target selection of the representative sample in Section~\ref{sec:data_samples}.

The reduced one-dimensional spectra are produced with the HERMES reduction pipeline of \citet{Kos2017}, updated for GALAH DR4. This pipeline performs the extraction, wavelength calibration, sky subtraction, telluric correction, barycentric correction, uncertainty estimation, and line-spread-function measurement for each spectrum. For each spectrum, GALAH DR4 provides the observed flux, the associated flux uncertainty, and the best-fitting synthetic spectrum inferred by the DR4 analysis and used to normalise the observation, as exemplified in Fig.~\ref{fig:example_spectrum_170710003201361}, where we visualise residuals in three different intensities based on their significance with respect to the reported flux uncertainty.

    \caption{Example GALAH DR4 spectrum of the red giant 2MASS~J22084160+0607286 (\texttt{sobject\_id} 170710003201361). In using these products, we retain the GALAH DR4 masking of the strongly telluric-contaminated region $7585$--$7680\,\text{\AA}$. A reliable treatment of this wavelength range requires dedicated telluric modelling alongside the stellar model, as demonstrated by \citet{Saydjari2023} and \citet{Casey2026}, whereas our aim is to assess residual structure in the DR4 analysis products rather than to introduce an additional telluric-modelling step.

The GALAH DR4 stellar labels were inferred from a hybrid spectrum-synthesis workflow. The synthetic spectra were computed with \textsc{Spectroscopy Made Easy} \citep[SME;][]{Valenti1996, Piskunov2017} and one-dimensional MARCS model atmospheres \citep{Gustafsson2008}, using the GALAH line list developed from the compilation of \citet{Heiter2021}. To make the analysis feasible across the full GALAH wavelength range, GALAH DR4 trains neural-network interpolators on synthetic spectra in local regions of $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ parameter space. These interpolators enable the simultaneous optimisation of global stellar parameters and elemental abundances across most of the observed wavelength range, including broad molecular C$_2$ and CN absorption features. The optimisation was done using up to 94\% of the pixels, with several pixels being excluded by default after initial tests indicated overall bad fit qualities for them \citep[for more details see Sec.~4.3.2 of][]{Buder2025b}. The 6\% of spectra that were synthesised but not used for optimisation are particularly interesting for this work and indicated as grey shaded regions in Fig.~\ref{fig:example_spectrum_170710003201361}.

The synthetic spectra include one-dimensional non-LTE line formation for atomic lines where available. In GALAH DR4, this treatment is implemented through departure-coefficient grids from the BALDER/Multi3D framework and associated model atoms for H, Li, C, N, O, Na, Mg, Al, Si, K, Ca, Mn, Fe, and Ba \citep[e.g.][]{Amarsi2020b, Amarsi2022}. Other atomic transitions and molecular features are treated in one-dimensional LTE. This distinction is important for the interpretation of residuals: although GALAH DR4 models the broad C$_2$ and CN absorption that affected earlier analyses, these molecular features do not yet have the same non-LTE treatment as the listed atomic transitions.

We use the GALAH DR4 labels as reference quantities against which residual structure can be tested. The main fitted stellar labels include the global stellar parameters $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$, microturbulence velocity $v_\mathrm{mic}$, projected rotational velocity $v\sin i$, and radial velocity $v_\mathrm{rad}$. We also use the reported elemental abundances for Li, C, N, O, Na, Mg, Al, Si, K, Ca, Sc, Ti, V, Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn, Rb, Sr, Y, Zr, Mo, Ru, Ba, La, Ce, Nd, Sm, and Eu. These are reported as $[\mathrm{X/Fe}]$ for most elements, with lithium reported as $A(\mathrm{Li})$. We use the associated abundance flags to distinguish reliable detections from upper limits, non-detections, and measurements affected by optimisation or boundary issues.

GALAH DR4 reports spectra on their native pixel grids, with individual wavelength solutions for each observed spectrum. Because the data-driven models used in this work require all spectra to be represented on a shared wavelength grid, we interpolate the observed fluxes, best-fitting synthetic fluxes, and flux uncertainties onto a common wavelength grid for each HERMES arm. The construction of the common grid for the representative sample is described in Section~\ref{sec:data_samples}.

We also make use of auxiliary quantities reported by GALAH DR4. These include extinction-related quantities such as $E(B-V)$ from the dust maps of \citet{Schlegel1998} and $A_\mathrm{K_S}$ estimated via the Rayleigh-Jeans Color Excess method \citep{Majewski2011}, as reported by \citet{Buder2025b}. These quantities are not stellar-atmosphere labels fitted by the spectral synthesis, but they provide useful reference labels for testing whether residual spectra contain information associated with line-of-sight dust and interstellar absorption. GALAH DR4 also reports Gaussian fits to residuals at the expected positions of interstellar K~{\sc i} and three diffuse interstellar bands \citep[see Sec.~6.1.3][]{Buder2025b}, as well as measurements of emission signals in the Balmer lines \citep[see Sec.~6.1.4][]{Buder2025b}. These are diagnostic products of the residual spectra and are not included in the stellar synthetic spectra used in the DR4 analysis.

\subsection{Parent sample and analysis samples}
\label{sec:data_samples}

For this pilot study, we select spectra observed during three consecutive GALAH survey nights between 10 and 12 July 2017. This choice provides a compact but representative test set of ordinary GALAH targets, rather than a sample selected directly by stellar parameters, abundance quality, or residual behaviour. The selected fields are restricted to 22 pointings of nominal GALAH fields, where stars are selected randomly within a magnitude range
(3 bright fields with $9 < V~/~\mathrm{mag} < 12$, %  2532, 3897, 4592
18 main fields with $12 < V~/~\mathrm{mag} < 14$, % 67, 89, 178, 252, 285, 638, 689, 930, 982, 2120, 2831, 3459, 4286, 4559, 5436, 5792, 5971, 6415
and one faint field with $9 < V~/~\mathrm{mag} < 14.3$) % 6966
and therefore provide a useful first data set for testing whether systematic residual structure can be recovered across the typical stellar-parameter space of the survey. Across the three nights, 7627 observations of unique stars have been taken without repeated pointing at the same star.

In the following subsections, we describe how we create three separate samples as input for our residual analysis: a \textit{baseline} sample (Sec.~\ref{sec:sample_baseline}) with some quality cuts, an \textit{abundance} sample with additional requirements for quality and abundance measurements (Sec.~\ref{sec:sample_abundance}), and finally an \textit{activity} sample for which we select only sources with available activity indicators from a literature source (Sec.~\ref{sec:sample_activity}).

\subsubsection{Baseline Sample}
\label{sec:sample_baseline}

We filter these observations with several quality criteria. For each, the DR4 pipeline needs to have converged to a fit solution (\texttt{flag\_sp} $<2048$) with reasonable fit-quality of $\chi^2_\mathrm{sp} < 2$. This cut primarily removes spectra with strong molecular features and poor global fits, especially the coolest giants.
We further require finite values of both $E(B-V)$ and $A_\mathrm{K_S}$ and exposures in all four CCDs (\texttt{snr\_px\_ccd} > 10).
We do not remove spectra with emission-line signatures or binary flags, because such spectra provide relevant tests of whether non-stellar or poorly modelled spectral structure is recovered by the residual analysis.
These criteria leave a baseline sample of 7087 spectra with median signal-to-noise ratios of 29, 42, 55, and 50 in the four CCDs respectively. Each spectrum is from a unique star and no repeat observations are taken across the nights.

The distribution of this sample in the Kiel diagram is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:labels_m1_teff_logg_feh}. The sample covers the main stellar types observed by GALAH, spanning approximately $3600 < T_\mathrm{eff}/\mathrm{K} < 8000$, $0.5 < \log(g/\mathrm{cm\,s^{-2}}) < 5.0$, and $-3.0 < \mathrm{[Fe/H]} < 0.75$ (but mainly $-0.5 < \mathrm{[Fe/H]} < 0.1$). The vast majority of stars are observed at low extinction, $A_{K_S} = 0.02_{-0.02}^{+0.09}\,\mathrm{mag}$ up to a maximum of $A_{K_S} \leq 0.37\,\mathrm{mag}$.

For the representative sample, we construct a shared wavelength grid separately for each HERMES arm. We define each grid using the largest starting wavelength, the median wavelength dispersion, and the smallest final wavelength among the selected spectra. This gives common wavelength ranges of
\begin{align}
    4719.0299..&0.0460..4893.8808\,\text{\AA}, \notag \\
    5655.5298..&0.0547..5863.0182\,\text{\AA}, \notag \\
    6486.0104..&0.0631..6726.4572\,\text{\AA}\text{, and} \notag \\
    7689.1261..&0.0735..7874.3127\,\text{\AA} \notag
\end{align}
for the blue, green, red, and near-infrared arms, respectively. We interpolate the observed flux, best-fitting synthetic flux, and flux uncertainty onto these grids before constructing the residual matrix.

We define the residual spectrum as the difference between the observed and best-fitting synthetic flux,
\begin{equation}
    \Delta f_\lambda = f_{\lambda\,\mathrm{obs}} - f_{\lambda\,\mathrm{model}}, \label{eq:def_residuals}
\end{equation}
and use the reported flux uncertainties $\sigma_{f,\lambda}$ to calculate a residual significance,
\begin{equation}
    s_\lambda = \frac{\vert \Delta f_\lambda \vert}{\sigma_{f,\lambda}}, \label{eq:def_significance}
\end{equation}
and to construct inverse-variance weights,
\begin{equation}
    w_\lambda = \sigma_{f,\lambda}^{-2}. \label{eq:def_ivar}
\end{equation}
After requiring finite interpolated fluxes and uncertainties across the common wavelength grid, the final matrix contains 7087 spectra and 13927 wavelength pixels. This residual matrix forms the input to the data-driven models analysed in the later sections.

\subsubsection{Abundance Sample}
\label{sec:sample_abundance}

Based on chemical evolution studies \citep[e.g.][]{Kobayashi2020}, we know that the chemical enhancement of several elements is strongly correlated due to their similar nucleosynthetic origins. As a result, the absorption lines of these elements are also expected to be strongly correlated. We will use this insight and representative elements of a range of nucleosynthesis or element groups to search for residual lines who are correlated with these enrichment patterns. In particular, we use Mg to represent core-collapse supernovae-dominated elements, Mn to represent supernovae Ia-dominated elements, Na to represent the behaviour of several odd-Z elements, and Ba to represent asymptotic giant branch star-dominated neutron-capture elements. We only select spectra from the \textit{baseline} sample which also fulfil $\texttt{flag\_x\_fe} = 0$ for $\texttt{x} \in \mathrm{[Na, Mg, Mn, Ba]}$. As we are searching for possibly smaller signals, we increase our sensitivity to such signals by restricting the sample to slow rotators with typically narrow absorption lines, where most of the absorption signal is focussed in fewer pixels of our GALAH spectra. We thus further restrict this sample to slow-rotating stars ($\texttt{vsini} < 15\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$) and also cut a few dozen stars (less than 1\%) when enforcing $T_\mathrm{eff} < 6500\,\mathrm{K}$. We show the distribution of stellar parameters for the resulting \textit{activity} sample of 3826 spectra Fig.~\ref{fig:labels_m2_teff_logg_feh_xfe}. For further information, we also show the neglected hot stars and fast rotators as grey crosses and an inset of one of the abundance distributions, [Fe/H] vs. [Mg/Fe].

Ideally, we would want to also include the r-process enrichment pattern. However, Eu, as the sole element in the GALAH range known to be strongly dominated by this enrichment patter, is not well modelled in GALAH DR4. Similary, Sm, as the next most dominated element is not detected often enough. And Nd, as the third most dominated element is already showing a very similar pattern to the remaining s-process elements, and would decrease the useful training set from 3826 to 2663 spectra (a decrease of 30 percent). In a second step, we aim to model the residuals with a data-driven approach as a function of a variety of labels (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon}).

\section{A systematic audit of spectrum residuals}
\label{sec:analysis_audit}

Before asking whether residual spectra contain recoverable information, we first examine the residuals themselves. This section provides a systematic audit of where the observed GALAH DR4 spectra and their best-fitting synthetic spectra disagree, how coherent these disagreements are across the baseline sample, and whether their amplitude, sign, width, and wavelength location point to plausible astrophysical, observational, or modelling origins. The goal is not to assign a unique physical explanation to every residual feature, but to establish an empirical residual map and catalogue that are reproducible, interpretable, and useful for guiding the data-driven modelling in Section~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon}.

The audit proceeds in three steps. First, we quantify the amplitude, significance, sign, recurrence, and wavelength dependence of the residuals across the baseline sample (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_significance}). Second, we define a reproducible catalogue of significant residual features using a transparent peak-finding procedure applied independently in each GALAH wavelength arm (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_peak_catalogue}). In this section, we also compare these residual features to external line identifications, GALAH DR4 line-list information, telluric features, and sky-line diagnostics to associate them with plausible classes of origin.

\subsection{Where do significant residuals occur?}
\label{sec:analysis_significance}

The first step of the residual audit is to establish whether the residuals are consistent with random noise, or whether they contain coherent structure across many spectra. Two properties are central for this: the significance of a residual relative to the flux uncertainty, and its sign. Negative residuals, $\Delta f_\lambda < 0$, indicate that the observed spectrum is deeper than the model and may point to missing opacity, underestimated blends, omitted lines, or imperfect line-formation physics. Positive residuals, $\Delta f_\lambda > 0$, indicate that the observed spectrum is shallower than the model and may point to over-predicted absorption, line-core filling, continuum-normalisation effects, or reduction systematics. Separating the two signs is therefore essential: an unsigned residual spectrum measures mismatch, but a signed residual spectrum helps diagnose its possible origin.

As a first global check, we compare the distribution of all residual significances in the baseline sample to the expectation for Gaussian noise. For normally distributed residuals with correctly estimated uncertainties, 68.3\% and $99.7\,\mathrm{\%}$ of residuals should lie within $1\sigma$ and $3\sigma$, respectively. Across the 99 million residual pixels in the baseline sample, only $63.4\,\mathrm{\%}$ and $95.3\,\mathrm{\%}$ lie within these limits. This corresponds to 4.6 million residual pixels outside $3\sigma$, compared to only 0.3 million expected for Gaussian noise. The residual field is therefore not fully described by the quoted observational uncertainties and random noise alone.

We next ask where these residuals occur in wavelength. Figure~\ref{fig:residual_statistics} summarises three complementary diagnostics across the four GALAH wavelength arms. The top panels show the median signed residual, together with the 16th and 84th percentiles, at each wavelength pixel. Most pixels have median residuals close to zero, but several regions show coherent offsets in flux space. Among the 14 strongest negative median residuals, with $\Delta f_\lambda < -0.1$, 11 occur in the blue arm, including the strongest features near 4748, 4771, and $4861\,\text{\AA}$ ($\mathrm{H}_\upbeta$). The remaining strong negative features occur near $5694\,\text{\AA}$, $\mathrm{H}_\upalpha$ at 6563\,\AA, and the region near $7800\,\text{\AA}$. Strong positive median residuals are slightly less frequent; the eight clearest positive features ($\Delta f_\lambda > 0.1$) occur near 4745, 4792, 4888, 4891, 5668, 5754, 5831, and $7827\,\text{\AA}$. In addition to these narrow features, we identify broader low-amplitude structures, including a wave-like residual pattern in CCD3 and broader residual behaviour in CCD1. These broad structures are less obvious in absolute flux, but may still be significant relative to the uncertainties.

The middle panels of Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics} show the median residual significance as a function of wavelength. This view highlights wavelength regions where the residuals are systematically large compared to the flux uncertainties, even when the median signed residual is modest. The cumulative distribution of per-wavelength median significances
% in Fig.~\ref{fig:cdf_significance_residual_flux_percentiles_m1} 
shows that most wavelength pixels are well behaved: $87.8\,\mathrm{\%}$ of pixels have a median residual significance below $1\sigma$, and $97.3\,\mathrm{\%}$ below $2\sigma$. The remaining tail, however, is precisely the important part for this audit. It identifies wavelength regions where the mismatch between observations and synthesis is not driven by a few individual outliers, but recurs across a substantial fraction of the baseline sample.

The typical level of residual significance also differs between the GALAH wavelength arms. CCD1 and CCD3 show slightly higher median residual significances than CCD2 and CCD4. This could indicate that the spectral synthesis performs differently across the arms, that the flux uncertainties are calibrated differently, or that some arms contain more difficult stellar, telluric, or reduction features. We therefore do not define significant residual regions using a single absolute threshold across all arms. Instead, in the next subsection, we identify outlying residual features relative to the median-significance distribution within each arm. Specifically, we use the 75th percentile of the median residual significance in each CCD as an arm-dependent reference level, corresponding to median significances of 0.92, 0.71, 0.87, and 0.67 for CCDs 1-4, respectively. These levels are shown as dashed horizontal lines in the middle panels of Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics}. Pixels above these thresholds (highlighted as blue regions) reveal both narrow residual peaks and broader residual structures, including the $\mathrm{H}_\upalpha$ region and broad structure around $7715\,\text{\AA}$.

Finally, we quantify how often residuals occur with a preferred sign. For each wavelength pixel, we calculate the fraction of the 7087 spectra in which the observed flux is significantly shallower or deeper than the synthesis. The bottom panels of Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics} show these signed recurrence fractions for the $3\sigma$ threshold. Most wavelength pixels have low recurrence fractions in both directions, but several features are significant in the same direction for a large fraction of the sample. We find nine wavelength regions where the observations are significantly shallower than the synthesis for more than $50\,\mathrm{\%}$ of spectra: 4745, 4792, 4891, 5668, 5754, 5831, 6593, 6713, and $7827\,\text{\AA}$. Conversely, we find 18 wavelength regions where the observations are significantly deeper than the synthesis for more than $50\,\mathrm{\%}$ of spectra: 4731, 4740, 4741, 4748, 4756, 4765, 4771, 4784, 4805, 4809, 4861, 5694, 5754, 5757, 6502, 6563, 6718, and $7800\,\text{\AA}$. The Balmer lines are dominated by residuals in which the observed spectra are deeper than the synthesis. Other regions, especially in CCD4, show broader residual structure with lower but noticeable recurrence in both directions, suggesting residual behaviour that may vary with stellar type, observing conditions, or reduction quality. The wave-like pattern in CCD3 is also visible in the signed recurrence diagnostic, although much of it remains below the $3\sigma$ threshold and is more apparent in the corresponding $1\sigma$ and $2\sigma$ panels shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:percentage_residuals_1_and_2_sigma}.

\textbf{Key Takeaway:} Several broad conclusions follow from Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics}. First, although most wavelength pixels are well modelled on average, the full residual distribution contains far more high-significance outliers than expected from Gaussian noise. Second, the most significant residuals are wavelength-localised rather than randomly distributed, with both narrow line-like features and broader coherent structures. Third, many residual features have a preferred sign, indicating systematic over- or under-prediction of absorption, while others appear in both directions and may depend on stellar labels, observing conditions, or reduction effects. These results motivate the next step of the audit: defining a reproducible catalogue of residual features that can be compared to stellar line identifications, interstellar features, telluric absorption, and sky diagnostics.

\subsection{A catalogue of residual peaks and their plausible causes}
\label{sec:analysis_peak_catalogue}

Having established that significant residuals are wavelength-localised, recurrent, and often signed, we now convert the residual statistics into a reproducible catalogue of candidate features. The purpose of this catalogue is not to provide a unique decomposition of the residual spectrum or a definitive physical identification of every feature. Rather, it provides a transparent set of significant residual regions that can be inspected, classified, and used to guide both their interpretation and future improvements to the spectral modelling.

We identify residual peaks from the median residual-significance spectrum of the \textit{baseline} sample. We use the median rather than the maximum significance so that the selection is driven by residuals recurring across many stars rather than by individual outliers. The search is performed independently in each GALAH wavelength arm using the \textsc{find\_peaks} function of \textsc{scipy} \citep{Scipy}. As motivated in Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_significance}, we adopt the \input{tex_text/tuned_threshold}th percentile of the median residual-significance distribution in each arm as an arm-dependent reference level. We additionally require a minimum peak width of \input{tex_text/tuned_peak_width.tex} pixels and a minimum separation of \input{tex_text/tuned_peak_distance.tex} pixels. These criteria suppress isolated single-pixel artefacts and reduce the subdivision of broad residual structures into multiple closely spaced detections. The resulting peak positions are shown as red dashed lines in the middle panels of Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics}.

This procedure identifies \input{tex_text/number_significant_peaks_ccd1}, \input{tex_text/number_significant_peaks_ccd2}, \input{tex_text/number_significant_peaks_ccd3}, and \input{tex_text/number_significant_peaks_ccd4} residual peaks in CCDs 1--4, respectively, for a total of 380 candidate residual regions. The precise number depends on the adopted threshold, width, and separation parameters. Our aim is therefore not to define a unique mathematical decomposition, but to construct a reproducible and interpretable catalogue of the most prominent residual structure.

For each residual region, we combine several diagnostics in a single inspection figure. The residual morphology is described by the median logarithmic significance and by the fractions of spectra in which the observation is significantly shallower or deeper than the synthesis. Predominantly negative residuals, for which the observed absorption is deeper, indicate that additional or stronger opacity is required. Predominantly positive residuals indicate that the synthesised absorption is too strong or that the observed line is partly filled in. Mixed-sign residuals suggest that the mismatch changes across stellar-label space or is affected by observational conditions. Broad residuals are treated with particular caution because telluric, sky, and reduction features defined in the observed frame can be smeared when the spectra are shifted into the stellar rest frame.

We compare each region with the Solar and Arcturus atlases of \citet{Hinkle2000}, including their stellar-line identifications and telluric measurements. We also compare against the GALAH DR4 line list, selecting atomic entries with estimated central depths of $\texttt{DEPTH}>0.1$ for identifying possible contributors and $\texttt{DEPTH}>0.85$ for visualisation \citep[see also Sec.~4.2 of][]{Heiter2021}. This does not constitute a complete list of all possible blends, but includes the transitions expected to be most visible in high-quality Solar and Arcturus spectra. We additionally compare broad or ambiguous regions against the catalogue of diffuse interstellar bands by \citet{Vogrincic2023} and against sky-emission calibration observations where available.

We assigned each region a preliminary classification using a deterministic decision tree based on its wavelength, width, recurrence, residual direction, and proximity to features in these external diagnostics. Broad features coincident with established diffuse interstellar bands or interstellar K~{\sc i} were classified as \textit{Interstellar}, while features coincident with sky emission were classified as \textit{Skyline}. Regions showing significant residuals in fewer than $5\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the spectra were labelled \textit{Insignificant}.

For the remaining regions, we searched for stellar transitions within one detector pixel of the residual peak. Where the \citet{Hinkle2000} atlas and the GALAH DR4 line list identified the same transition, residuals that were predominantly shallower or deeper than the synthesis were classified as candidate \textit{$\log gf$ too high} or \textit{$\log gf$ too low}, respectively. Features present in the atlas but absent from the GALAH DR4 line list were classified as \textit{Known missing}, while features without a counterpart in either source were classified as \textit{Unknown line}. Regions with asymmetric, split, or otherwise structured profiles that could not be explained by a simple change in line strength were assigned to \textit{Line profile}. The \textit{Unclear} category was retained for blended or ambiguous cases for which no single explanation was sufficiently supported.

We subsequently inspected all 380 regions visually. The preliminary classification was confirmed for 312 regions ($82\,\mathrm{\%}$), while 68 classifications ($18\,\mathrm{\%}$) were revised. Most revisions involved blended or conflicting line identifications, Balmer-line or hyperfine-structure residuals, the broad undulating pattern in CCD3, ambiguous skyline coincidences, or cases in which the residual morphology indicated a profile mismatch rather than a simple line-strength error.

The final catalogue is dominated by candidate stellar line-strength mismatches. We classify 227 regions as \textit{$\log gf$ too high} (129 regions) or \textit{$\log gf$ too low} (98 regions), with a further 13 regions assigned to \textit{Line profile}. Together, these 236 regions account for $62\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the catalogue. Another 46 regions correspond to absent or unidentified absorption features, comprising 8 \textit{Known missing} and 38 \textit{Unknown line} classifications. We identify 15 \textit{Skyline} and 12 \textit{Interstellar} regions, while 19 cases remain \textit{Unclear}. The remaining 51 regions are classified as \textit{Insignificant}, most commonly because the apparent peak is significant in only a small fraction of the sample or forms part of broader detector-scale structure.

Figure~\ref{fig:example_significant_residual_regions} illustrates the nine adopted classifications. Panels A--C show three forms of stellar-modelling mismatch: a line that is too strong in the synthesis, a line that is too weak, and a structured profile mismatch. Panels D--F show a low-recurrence or detector-scale feature, residual sky emission, and interstellar absorption. Panels G--I illustrate progressively less certain identifications: an unidentified feature, a known stellar line absent from the GALAH DR4 line list, and an ambiguous blend for which several explanations remain plausible.

Fe~{\sc i} accounts for the largest number of candidate line-strength mismatches, including 41 of the 129 \textit{$\log gf$ too high} regions and 36 of the 98 \textit{$\log gf$ too low} regions. Recurrent classifications are also found for Ti~{\sc i}, Ce~{\sc ii}, Cr~{\sc i}, Si~{\sc i}, V~{\sc i}, Ca~{\sc i}, and Ni~{\sc i}. These counts are descriptive rather than occurrence rates because the number and strength of available transitions vary substantially between species. The eight \textit{Known missing} features comprise four Cr~{\sc i}, three Fe~{\sc i}, and one Si~{\sc i} transition.

The classifications represent the most plausible explanation supported by the available diagnostics rather than unique physical identifications. In particular, the categories \textit{$\log gf$ too high} and \textit{$\log gf$ too low} indicate the direction in which the synthesised line strength would need to change. The underlying cause could instead involve an unresolved blend, inaccurate abundance, incomplete hyperfine or isotopic structure, wavelength or broadening errors, or deficiencies in the adopted line-formation treatment. The \textit{Line profile}, \textit{Unknown line}, and \textit{Unclear} classes explicitly identify cases for which a simple adjustment of the oscillator strength would be insufficient or poorly justified.

A complete version of Tab.~\ref{tab:tabular_peak_information}, including the classifications, supporting measurements, and inspection comments for all 380 regions, is provided as a machine-readable FITS table in the public repository\footnote{\url{https://github.com/svenbuder/residual_cannon/blob/main/data/galah_dr4_residual_peaks.fits}}. The corresponding diagnostic figure for every region is also provided in the repository.

\textbf{Key Takeaway:} The significant residuals are not an unstructured collection of modelling failures. Most can be associated with a plausible and potentially actionable cause, with candidate stellar line-strength and line-profile mismatches accounting for $63\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the catalogue. The catalogue also identifies missing and unidentified absorption features, interstellar absorption, and residual sky emission, thereby providing a prioritised set of wavelength regions for improving the spectral modelling.

\section{Data-driven extraction of residual information}
\label{sec:analysis_cannon}

The systematic audit in Section~\ref{sec:analysis_audit} shows that the residual spectra contain coherent, recurrent structure with several plausible stellar, interstellar, and observational origins. We now ask whether this structure is predictive: can the mismatch between an observed spectrum and its best-fitting model be used to recover properties of the star or observation? To test this, we apply the data-driven framework of \textsc{The Cannon}, described in Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon_method}, to the residual spectra themselves rather than to the observed spectra.

We first test whether residuals retain information about stellar parameters and elemental abundances already included in the GALAH fit (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon_fitted_labels}). We then examine properties not fitted by the synthesis, focusing on interstellar extinction and chromospheric activity (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon_unfitted_labels}). Finally, we compare the information retained in wavelength pixels used during the GALAH DR4 optimisation with that in synthesised pixels masked from the fit (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon_masked_pixels}).

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Residual modelling and validation with \textsc{The Cannon}}
\label{sec:analysis_cannon_method}

We model the residual spectra using \textsc{The Cannon} \citep{Ness2015} with an updated version\footnote{We use v.0.9.00 from \url{https://github.com/ANU-RSAA/AnniesLasso}, maintained by Marc White at the ANU Research School of Astronomy and Astrophysics.} of the code by \citet{Casey2016}. Unlike its conventional application, in which \textsc{The Cannon} learns the relationship between observed flux and stellar labels, here we train it on the residual flux between each observed spectrum and its best-fitting synthetic spectrum.

For a star $n$ at pixel $\lambda$, we model each residual $\Delta f_{n\lambda}$ as
\begin{equation}
\Delta f_{n\lambda}
=
\boldsymbol{v}(\boldsymbol{\ell}_n) \cdot \boldsymbol{\theta}_{\lambda} + \epsilon_{n\lambda},
\label{eq:cannon_residual_model}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\ell}_n$ contains the selected labels of star $n$, $\boldsymbol{v}(\boldsymbol{\ell}_n)$ is the corresponding design vector, and $\epsilon_{n\lambda}$ is a noise term drawn from a Gaussian distribution whose variance combines the observational flux uncertainty $\sigma_{n\lambda}$ and an additional wavelength-dependent model scatter $\sigma_{\mathrm{model},\lambda}$,
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_{n\lambda} \sim \mathcal{N}\left(0, \sigma_{n\lambda}^{2}+\sigma_{\mathrm{model},\lambda}^{2}\right).
\end{equation}

Throughout this work, we use a quadratic model, for which the design vector contains a constant term, the linear and quadratic terms of each label, and all pairwise cross terms. The wavelength-dependent coefficient vector $\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\lambda}$ therefore describes how the residual flux changes across label space. For numerical stability, \textsc{The Cannon} internally centres and scales the input labels before constructing the quadratic design vector.

We train the model coefficients and scatter at each wavelength pixel using stars with known reference labels. For a new residual spectrum, we then fix these quantities and infer its labels by maximising the joint likelihood across all wavelength pixels, equivalent to minimising the summed $\chi^2$.

The models used in this section differ in their training sample, input labels, and wavelength coverage. They test whether residuals retain information about stellar parameters, abundance patterns, extinction, and activity, and whether that information is concentrated in pixels used or masked during the GALAH DR4 optimisation, as already listed in Tab.~\ref{tab:residual_models}.

We analysed the robustness of our data-driven extraction through five-fold cross-validation. Using the \textsc{KFold} implementation of \textsc{sklearn}'s \textsc{model\_selection} \citep{scikit-learn}, we divided each sample into five reproducibly shuffled subsets. For each split, we trained the model on $80\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the spectra and evaluated it on the remaining $20\,\mathrm{\%}$, such that every spectrum was used for testing once.

We quantify the recovery of each label using
\begin{equation}
\Delta \ell
=
\ell_{\mathrm{Cannon}}
-
\ell_{\mathrm{reference}}.
\label{eq:label_residual}
\end{equation}
In Figs.~\ref{fig:recovery_comparison}, \ref{fig:recovery_comparison2}, and \ref{fig:recovery_comparison3}, we visualise the recovery performance for a selection\footnote{All remaining plots can be found in our \href{https://github.com/svenbuder/residual_cannon}{repository}.} of the relevant labels (see later subsections). For each, we report the median label and its 16th--84th percentile range $\ell$ in the top left, together with the median bias and the 16th--84th percentile range of $\Delta \ell$ in the top right. In addition to the percentiles, which allow us to identify asymmetries, and to better assess recovery performance with respect to the label range, we report both the range of a label (represented by the standard deviation $\sigma(\ell)$), as well as the standard deviation of their recovery, $\sigma{(\Delta \ell})$, together with their ratio
\begin{equation}
    R_\ell = \frac{\sigma(\ell)}{\sigma{(\Delta \ell})}
\end{equation}
in the bottom of each figure. In all cases, we use the more robust estimation of standard deviation as half the distribution between 16th and 84th percentile.

These performance indicators, in particular a higher ratio $R_\ell$, establish whether the residuals contain predictive information. The wavelength-dependent linear coefficients (Figs.~\ref{fig:cannon_linear_coefficients_joint} and \ref{fig:cannon_linear_coefficients_model5}) are then used to identify where that information resides and to compare the learned residual sensitivity with known spectral lines and the residual catalogue from Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_peak_catalogue}. For each wavelength pixel, we show the median coefficient across the five folds together with its full fold-to-fold range. Because each fold contains 80\% of the sample, the internal label scaling and resulting coefficients differ only slightly. The narrow envelopes for almost all linear coefficients therefore demonstrate that the learned wavelength dependence is robust to the choice of training subset; they are not interpreted as formal coefficient uncertainties.

\subsection{Information about fitted stellar labels}
\label{sec:analysis_cannon_fitted_labels}

The GALAH synthesis was explicitly optimised using global stellar parameters and individual elemental abundances. As a reference scale, the median uncertainties reported by GALAH DR4 for stars in the \textit{abundance} sample are $70\,\mathrm{K}$ in $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $0.10\,\mathrm{dex}$ in $\log g$, and $0.060\,\mathrm{dex}$ in $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$, which all include absolute uncertainties as well. For the elemental abundances, the corresponding median precision uncertainties are $0.035\,\mathrm{dex}$ for $[\mathrm{Na/Fe}]$, $0.029\,\mathrm{dex}$ for $[\mathrm{Mg/Fe}]$, $0.031\,\mathrm{dex}$ for $[\mathrm{Mn/Fe}]$, and $0.068\,\mathrm{dex}$ for $[\mathrm{Ba/Fe}]$.

Recovering these labels from the residuals would therefore show that the remaining model-data mismatch still varies systematically across the label space used in the original fit. We first examine the global stellar parameters in Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon_fitted_stellar_parameters}, before testing for more specific information about elemental abundance patterns in Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon_fitted_abundances}.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsubsection{Stellar parameters}
\label{sec:analysis_cannon_fitted_stellar_parameters}

Model \textcolor{M1}{M1} tests the recovery of $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ using the baseline sample, which spans the broadest stellar-parameter range considered here. Model \textcolor{M1}{M1*} repeats the same experiment using the more restricted abundance sample, which excludes the hottest stars and applies additional quality criteria. This comparison tests whether the predictive residual structure persists within a more homogeneous, higher-quality sample, although it does not isolate the effects of signal-to-noise ratio, quality selection, and stellar-type coverage individually.

The cross-validated recovery results are combined in Fig.~\ref{fig:recovery_comparison} using model-specific colour maps; here, we focus on the grey-scale panels for Model~\textcolor{M1}{M1}. For this model, the robust recovery scales are $\sigma(\Delta T_\mathrm{eff})=150\,\mathrm{K}$, $\sigma(\Delta\log g)=0.19\,\mathrm{dex}$, and $\sigma(\Delta[\mathrm{Fe/H}])=0.086\,\mathrm{dex}$. These are substantially smaller than the corresponding label spreads of $660\,\mathrm{K}$, $0.94\,\mathrm{dex}$, and $0.286\,\mathrm{dex}$, yielding label-recovery ratios of $R_{T_\mathrm{eff}}=4.5$, $R_{\log g}=4.9$, and $R_{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]}=3.3$. The median biases are small: $-20\,\mathrm{K}$, $0.00\,\mathrm{dex}$, and $-0.006\,\mathrm{dex}$, respectively.

The recovery is not uniform across the sampled label space. For $T_\mathrm{eff}$, the scatter and frequency of outliers increase among the comparatively sparse stars hotter than approximately $6500\,\mathrm{K}$. The $\log g$ recovery shows a mildly asymmetric trend among dwarfs near $\log g\sim4$ and increased scatter around the red clump near $\log g\sim2.5$. The metallicity recovery is strongest across the well-sampled central range and deteriorates towards the metal-poor edge, where few training stars are available. The quoted global recovery scales should therefore be interpreted as cross-validated interpolation performance within the baseline sample, rather than as uniform precision across its full parameter range.

Model \textcolor{M1}{M1*} confirms that the residual information persists in the more restricted abundance sample. Its robust recovery scales are $80\,\mathrm{K}$ in $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $0.14\,\mathrm{dex}$ in $\log g$, and $0.059\,\mathrm{dex}$ in $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$, compared with label spreads of $623\,\mathrm{K}$, $0.95\,\mathrm{dex}$, and $0.270\,\mathrm{dex}$. The corresponding ratios increase to $R_{T_\mathrm{eff}}=7.8$, $R_{\log g}=6.7$, and $R_{[\mathrm{Fe/H}]}=4.6$. The median biases remain negligible at $+1\,\mathrm{K}$, $0.00\,\mathrm{dex}$, and $-0.001\,\mathrm{dex}$. Model \textcolor{M1}{M1*} therefore performs better in both absolute terms and relative to the label coverage of its sample, although the combined sample-selection differences prevent us from attributing this improvement to any single factor. The complete recovery statistics are summarised in Tab.~\ref{tab:recovery_performance}.

The coefficient spectra panels in Fig.~\ref{fig:cannon_linear_coefficients_joint}, each showing the full range of coefficients across the five folds, identify the wavelength regions associated with this predictive information. Because the model is quadratic and the labels are internally centred and scaled, the plotted linear coefficients describe the local residual response near the centre of the training-set label distribution, conditional on the other labels. They should not be interpreted as global derivatives across the full parameter range; quadratic and cross terms may become important elsewhere in label space.

The $T_\mathrm{eff}$ coefficient contains numerous narrow positive and negative features across all four wavelength arms, indicating that the temperature dependence is distributed across many residual spectral features. The strongest response occurs around $\mathrm{H}_\upalpha$, where the line-core and wing residuals vary in opposite directions with temperature. This demonstrates a systematic temperature dependence in the remaining $\mathrm{H}_\upalpha$ mismatch, but does not by itself distinguish between deficiencies in line formation, broadening, continuum normalisation, activity, or other modelling effects. A lower-amplitude, broader response is also visible across parts of the blue arm, potentially reflecting the combined influence of many weaker features.

The $\log g$ coefficient is likewise dominated by structure around $\mathrm{H}_\upalpha$ and $\mathrm{H}_\upbeta$, together with narrower responses associated with individual metal-line residuals. These include regions near the modelled Fe~{\sc i} lines at 4870.97 and 4891.16\,\AA\ with too high $\log gf$ and the candidate missing Fe~{\sc i} line near 7780.56\,\AA. Parts of the blue-arm response have the opposite sign to the temperature coefficient, illustrating the partial covariance of the residual dependences on $T_\mathrm{eff}$ and $\log g$.

The $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ coefficient contains many line-localised responses, consistent with metallicity information distributed across absorption features and blends that are not reproduced perfectly by the synthesis. Not every Fe-associated peak from Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_peak_catalogue} appears prominently in the linear metallicity coefficient. Some features may instead vary non-linearly with metallicity, enter through cross terms with $T_\mathrm{eff}$ or $\log g$, or become informative only within a restricted region of label space.

Three related diagnostics should be distinguished. The cross-validated recovery scales quantify predictive performance for out-of-sample spectra, the inferred wavelength-dependent model scatter describes the typically small residual variation not captured by the fitted label model, and the fold-to-fold coefficient ranges measure the stability of the learned wavelength response against changes in the training subset. The coefficient envelopes in Fig.~\ref{fig:cannon_linear_coefficients_joint} are narrow for almost all linear terms, showing that the identified wavelength structure is highly reproducible across the five folds.

\textbf{Key Takeaway:} Residual spectra retain substantial and reproducible information about $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$, even though these labels were already used to construct the best-fitting synthetic spectra. The information is distributed across numerous spectral features, with particularly strong parameter-dependent residual structure around the Balmer lines, demonstrating that not only the flux, but also the remaining model-data mismatch varies coherently across stellar-label space.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsubsection{Element abundance patterns}
\label{sec:analysis_cannon_fitted_abundances}

Having established that the residual spectra retain substantial information about the global stellar parameters, we next test whether they preserve more specific chemical information. Model~\textcolor{M2}{M2} extends Model~\textcolor{M1}{M1*} by fitting $[\mathrm{Na/Fe}]$, $[\mathrm{Mg/Fe}]$, $[\mathrm{Mn/Fe}]$, and $[\mathrm{Ba/Fe}]$ alongside $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$. Both models use the same \textit{abundance} sample, allowing their performance to be compared without changing the underlying stellar sample. The recovery of the three global labels remains nearly unchanged, with robust scales of approximately $80\,\mathrm{K}$ in $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $0.15\,\mathrm{dex}$ in $\log g$, and $0.059\,\mathrm{dex}$ in $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ (Tab.~\ref{tab:recovery_performance}).

In contrast to the strong recovery of these global labels, the individual abundance ratios are recovered only modestly relative to their intrinsic sample spreads. The abundance recovery is shown by the blue-density panels in Fig.~\ref{fig:recovery_comparison}. The robust recovery scales $\sigma(\Delta[\mathrm{X/Fe}])$ are $0.084\,\mathrm{dex}$ for Na, $0.070\,\mathrm{dex}$ for Mg, $0.058\,\mathrm{dex}$ for Mn, and $0.14\,\mathrm{dex}$ for Ba. These values are larger than the corresponding median GALAH DR4 precision uncertainties by factors of approximately $1.9-2.4$.

The abundance recovery must also be considered relative to the limited label spread of the sample. The robust spreads $\sigma(\mathrm{[X/Fe]})$ are $0.102\,\mathrm{dex}$ for Na, $0.117\,\mathrm{dex}$ for Mg, $0.092\,\mathrm{dex}$ for Mn, and $0.16\,\mathrm{dex}$ for Ba. The corresponding label-recovery ratios are therefore $R_{[\mathrm{Na/Fe}]}=1.2$, $R_{[\mathrm{Mg/Fe}]}=1.7$, $R_{[\mathrm{Mn/Fe}]}=1.6$, and $R_{[\mathrm{Ba/Fe}]}=1.1$. The residual spectra thus contain modest predictive information about Mg and Mn, whereas Na and Ba are recovered only marginally better than a constant prediction near the centre of their training-label distributions. The recovery also deteriorates towards sparsely sampled abundance extremes, where individual training folds contain relatively few chemically enhanced stars.

The linear coefficients (blue in Fig.~\ref{fig:cannon_linear_coefficients_joint}) suggest that this abundance information is not carried primarily by clear residual signatures of the corresponding elements. In particular, the Mg and Ba coefficients retain broad, wave-like structure across the red arm, while several abundance coefficients respond strongly around the Balmer lines. The Mn and Ba coefficients also show structure near the diffuse interstellar bands around $5780\,\text{\AA}$, $5797\,\text{\AA}$, and $6613\,\text{\AA}$. By contrast, no single set of element-specific absorption features clearly dominates the coefficient spectrum for each abundance.

Because these coefficients describe conditional responses within a correlated, quadratic label model, such behaviour does not imply that the identified Balmer or interstellar features are directly sensitive to Na, Mg, Mn, or Ba. Instead, \textsc{The Cannon} can exploit residual structures associated with stellar populations, extinction, atmospheric parameters, or other properties that correlate with the abundance labels in the training sample. The abundance recovery therefore demonstrates that the residual spectra retain information about chemical abundance patterns, but not necessarily independent or directly attributable information about each individual element.

As for the stellar-parameter coefficients, the fold-to-fold envelopes are narrow relative to the median abundance-coefficient structure. The learned correlations are therefore reproducible across the five training subsets, even where their physical origin is indirect or ambiguous.

\textbf{Key Takeaway:} Residual spectra retain modest predictive information about elemental abundance patterns, with the strongest relative recovery for Mg and Mn. However, the abundance coefficients are not dominated by unambiguous element-specific residual features and instead include broad, Balmer-line, and interstellar-associated structure. The recovered abundance information therefore appears to arise at least partly through correlations with other stellar, interstellar, or observational properties.

\subsection{Information about properties not fitted by the synthesis}
\label{sec:analysis_cannon_unfitted_labels}

The abundance results show that predictive recovery does not have to arise from direct sensitivity to the corresponding atomic features. We therefore turn to properties not included in the GALAH synthesis, for which independent spectral diagnostics can help distinguish physically meaningful responses from indirect or contaminating correlations. We first examine interstellar extinction and absorption in Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon_extinction_labels}, followed by chromospheric activity in Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon_activity_labels}.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsubsection{Interstellar extinction and absorption}
\label{sec:analysis_cannon_extinction_labels}

Model~\textcolor{M3}{M3} extends Model~\textcolor{M1}{M1} by adding the line-of-sight extinction $A_{K_\mathrm{S}}$ to $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$, using the same \textit{baseline} sample. Although extinction does not directly alter a continuum-normalised stellar spectrum through its broad wavelength dependence, it correlates with narrow absorption produced by intervening interstellar material, including diffuse interstellar bands (DIBs) and atomic K~{\sc i}.

The baseline sample is strongly concentrated towards low-extinction sightlines, with $A_{K_\mathrm{S}} = 0.017_{-0.017}^{+0.054}\,\mathrm{mag}$ and the orange-density panel in Fig.~\ref{fig:recovery_comparison} therefore probes a highly asymmetric label distribution with a hard lower boundary near zero. 
The robust recovery scale, $\sigma(\Delta A_{K_\mathrm{S}})=0.037\,\mathrm{mag}$, is comparable to the label spread of $\sigma(A_{K_\mathrm{S}})=0.035\,\mathrm{mag}$, yielding $R_{A_{K_\mathrm{S}}}=1.0$. For such a narrow and strongly skewed distribution, however, this ratio is not by itself a reliable measure of predictive information: most stars have negligible extinction, for which both the reference and recovered values naturally cluster near zero. The absolute recovery differences generally remain below $0.1\,\mathrm{mag}$, including for the more extinguished sightlines, and stars with $A_{K_\mathrm{S}}\sim0.2\,\mathrm{mag}$ are preferentially assigned larger recovered values. The residual spectra therefore retain useful information about extinction, particularly towards the higher-extinction tail, even though the global recovery ratio is compressed by the label distribution.

The linear $A_{K_\mathrm{S}}$ coefficient in Fig.~\ref{fig:cannon_linear_coefficients_joint} provides clearer evidence for the physical origin of this information. Its strongest negative responses coincide with the established DIBs near $5780.59$, $6613.66$, and $5797.19\,\text{\AA}$, with peak linear-coefficient amplitudes $\delta (\Delta f) / \delta A_\mathrm{K_S}$ of approximately $-0.078$, $-0.045$, and $-0.030$, respectively. A similarly strong response near $7698.96\,\text{\AA}$, with an amplitude of approximately $-0.046$, coincides with interstellar K~{\sc i}. Additional responses align with DIBs identified by \citet{Vogrincic2023} near $4762.57$, $5705.21$, $6493.11$, $6496.67$, $6530.17$, and $6592.53\,\text{\AA}$. The agreement in wavelength and profile morphology shows that Model~\textcolor{M3}{M3} extracts identifiable interstellar absorption rather than only broad correlations between extinction and the stellar sample.

A complete one-to-one correspondence with the DIB catalogue of \citet{Vogrincic2023} is not expected. Their analysis shifted inferred ISM spectra into a common interstellar rest frame and combined high-quality spectra in reddening bins, whereas our coefficients are inferred from individual spectra in the stellar rest frame and from a sample dominated by nearly unreddened sightlines. Differences between the stellar and interstellar radial velocities can therefore broaden or dilute weak ISM responses in our analysis. This likely contributes to the absence of clear coefficients at several previously identified DIBs, including the features near $5747.62$ and $5784.78\,\text{\AA}$, as well as numerous weaker catalogue entries.

Conversely, Model~\textcolor{M3}{M3} shows additional responses near $4726.40$, $4771.47$, $4823.13$, $5849.91$, $6535.89$, $6660.74$, and $6699.33\,\text{\AA}$. These correlations should not automatically be classified as DIBs, because an extinction label can also trace stellar-population, observing, or reduction-related structure. This is particularly apparent for the narrow or step-like responses near $7737.34$, $7741.97$, $7747.26$, and $7826.79\,\text{\AA}$. Their morphology differs from the smoother and broader response (above 9 pixels), for example around the $5780\,\text{\AA}$ DIB (covering 29 pixels), making an interstellar origin less likely despite their correlation with $A_{K_\mathrm{S}}$.

The K~{\sc i} response near $7699\,\text{\AA}$ is also more complex than the isolated DIBs. The stellar synthesis includes the stellar photospheric K~{\sc i} line, while the observed spectrum may contain an additional interstellar component at a different radial velocity. When the stellar and interstellar components overlap, part of the additional absorption may be absorbed into the fitted stellar potassium abundance; when they are offset, the interstellar component remains as asymmetric or split residual structure around the synthesised stellar line. The coefficient should therefore be interpreted as evidence for interstellar K~{\sc i}, but not as a simple isolated line profile. We note that this behaviour might apply to several interstellar absorption features that overlap with fitted stellar absorption features.

\textbf{Key Takeaway:} Despite the strongly skewed distribution of $A_{K_\mathrm{S}}$, the residual spectra retain measurable information about interstellar extinction, with recovery differences generally remaining below $0.1\,\mathrm{mag}$ and the more extinguished sightlines preferentially assigned larger values. The linear coefficient spectrum provides the clearest physical evidence, recovering the strongest known DIBs and interstellar K~{\sc i}. Residual spectra therefore preserve identifiable information about intervening interstellar material that was not included in the stellar synthesis.

\subsubsection{Chromospheric activity}
\label{sec:analysis_cannon_activity_labels}

Model~\textcolor{M4}{M4} tests whether residual spectra retain information about chromospheric activity by fitting the activity indicator $\log R'_\mathrm{HK}$ alongside $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ for the 226 stars in the \textit{activity} sample. Extreme positive flux residuals, several of which arise from imperfectly subtracted sky emission, can strongly influence a model trained on such a small sample. We therefore cap continuum-normalised fluxes above 1.2 at this value and assign them an uncertainty of the original flux value, thereby strongly reducing their weight. As shown below, this treatment limits but does not eliminate correlations with sky-subtraction residuals.

Figure~\ref{fig:recovery_comparison2} contrasts the in-sample fit obtained when Model~\textcolor{M4}{M4} is trained and evaluated on all 226 spectra with its out-of-sample performance from five-fold cross-validation. The in-sample fit yields a robust recovery scale of $\sigma(\Delta\log R'_\mathrm{HK})=0.053$, compared with a label spread of $\sigma(\log R'_\mathrm{HK})=0.114$, corresponding to $R_{\log R'_\mathrm{HK}}=2.2$. This apparent recovery does not persist out of sample: the cross-validated recovery scale increases to $0.11$, giving $R_{\log R'_\mathrm{HK}}=1.0$. The diagonal trend in $\Delta\log R'_\mathrm{HK}$ indicates that the out-of-sample predictions regress towards the centre of the activity distribution rather than recovering its full range.

The contrast between the two results shows that the strong in-sample performance is not a reliable estimate of predictive precision. With only approximately 80 percent of the 226 spectra available in each training fold, the quadratic model does not generalise reliably to the left-out stars. This may reflect the small sample, its restricted and uneven activity coverage, sensitivity to individual spectra, or correlations with unrelated residual features. The experiment therefore does not establish reliable star-by-star recovery of $\log R'_\mathrm{HK}$ from the residuals with the current sample and model configuration.

The coefficient spectra nevertheless provide evidence that the residuals contain physically plausible activity-related structure. Figure~\ref{fig:logRHK_windows} examines selected regions in greater detail by showing the observed spectra of the 15 stars with the largest $\log R'_\mathrm{HK}$ values together with the linear and quadratic activity coefficients. The three most active stars show particularly clear changes around H$\upalpha$ and H$\upbeta$, supporting an activity-related origin for the Balmer-line coefficients. They also differ around the Li~{\sc i} line at $6707.8\,\text{\AA}$. Because lithium is primarily associated with stellar youth \citep[see e.g.][]{Zerjal2019}, rather than being a direct tracer of chromospheric emission, this response is more plausibly interpreted as a correlation between youth and activity within the sample.

Of the activity-sensitive lines identified by \citet{Wise2018} from HARPS spectra, four lie within the GALAH wavelength range: features near $4827.46$, $4861.33$, $5707.00$, and $6562.81\,\text{\AA}$ (see Fig.~\ref{fig:logRHK_windows}). The latter two Balmer lines, H$\upbeta$ and H$\upalpha$, show clear responses in the $\log R'_\mathrm{HK}$ coefficients, whereas the features near $4827.46$ and $5707.00\,\text{\AA}$ show no similarly prominent response. Among the 32 HARPS lines studied by \citet{Lisogorskyi2019} within our wavelength range (attached in Fig.~\ref{fig:logRHK_lines_Lisogorskyi2019}), we also identify a signal near $4736.78$, $4859.75$, and $4890.76\,\text{\AA}$, in addition to the Balmer lines.

Not all strong coefficient responses have a stellar origin. Model~\textcolor{M4}{M4} also responds to residual sky-emission features, including those near $7751.1$, $7794.7$, $7822.1$, and $7853.9\,\text{\AA}$ (also shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:logRHK_windows}). Some of these responses vary substantially between the cross-validation folds, indicating that they are driven by a small number of influential spectra rather than by a stable relation with chromospheric activity. Their presence illustrates that a reproducible correlation between a residual feature and an activity label does not by itself establish a causal connection: activity can correlate accidentally with observing conditions, reduction artefacts, or sample selection.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Where is the residual information concentrated?}
\label{sec:analysis_cannon_masked_pixels}

The preceding models of our data-driven analyses use all 13927 available wavelength pixels. To determine where the remaining label-dependent information is concentrated, we divide the spectrum into the 793 pixels excluded from the GALAH DR4 optimisation and the 13134 pixels retained for the fit. Model~\textcolor{M6}{M6} uses only the excluded pixels, corresponding to approximately $6\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the wavelength range, while Model~\textcolor{M6}{M6} uses the remaining $94\,\mathrm{\%}$. Both models fit $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ for the same 7087 spectra and use the same cross-validation folds as Model~\textcolor{M1}{M1}.

Figure~\ref{fig:recovery_comparison3} shows that both pixel subsets retain substantial stellar parameter information. Model~\textcolor{M6}{M6}, despite using only 793 pixels, recovers $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ with robust scales of approximately $160\,\mathrm{K}$, $0.22\,\mathrm{dex}$, and $0.095\,\mathrm{dex}$, respectively. These values are only moderately larger than the corresponding results of $150\,\mathrm{K}$, $0.19\,\mathrm{dex}$, and $0.086\,\mathrm{dex}$ obtained by Model~\textcolor{M1}{M1} from the complete wavelength range.

Model~\textcolor{M6}{M6}, which uses the 13134 pixels included in the GALAH DR4 optimisation, yields recovery scales of approximately $190\,\mathrm{K}$, $0.22\,\mathrm{dex}$, and $0.12\,\mathrm{dex}$. Relative to the label spreads of the baseline sample, the label-recovery ratios decrease from approximately $4.5$, $4.9$, and $3.3$ for Model~\textcolor{M1}{M1} to $4.1$, $4.4$, and $3.0$ for Model~\textcolor{M6}{M6}, and to $3.5$, $4.3$, and $2.5$ for Model~\textcolor{M6}{M6}. The complete-spectrum model therefore performs best, as expected, but the excluded pixels alone retain almost as much predictive information and outperform the much larger set of optimisation pixels for all three labels.

This comparison is not equivalent to selecting two random wavelength subsets. The 793 pixels were excluded from the GALAH DR4 optimisation because they were considered less reliable for the stellar-label fit, often owing to poorer model--data agreement or contamination. They are consequently more likely to contain systematic residual structure. Conversely, the pixels retained for the optimisation are precisely those for which the synthesis was considered more reliable and was adjusted to match the observed spectra. The stronger recovery from Model~\textcolor{M6}{M6} is therefore consistent with stellar-label information being concentrated in wavelength regions where the synthesis leaves the largest systematic mismatches.

The coefficient spectra in Fig.~\ref{fig:cannon_linear_coefficients_model5} support this interpretation. Many of the strongest narrow coefficient features identified by Model~\textcolor{M1}{M1} are also present when the model is restricted to the excluded pixels. These features therefore carry a substantial fraction of the residual information about $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$. A notable difference is that the broader responses around the Balmer lines are not fully represented by Model~\textcolor{M6}{M6}, because much of this extended wavelength structure lies outside the excluded-pixel mask. The narrow fold-to-fold ranges nevertheless show that the coefficient structure inferred from the 793 pixels is stable across the cross-validation splits.

The comparison also highlights that the number of pixels alone is not a useful measure of the information remaining in a residual spectrum. Model~\textcolor{M6}{M6} uses approximately 17 times fewer pixels than Model~\textcolor{M6}{M6}, yet achieves comparable or better out-of-sample recovery. The pixels excluded from the original optimisation therefore contain a disproportionately large amount of label-dependent residual structure.

\textbf{Key Takeaway:} The 6 percent of wavelength pixels excluded from the GALAH DR4 optimisation retain almost as much information about $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ as the complete residual spectrum, and more than the remaining 94 percent of optimisation pixels alone. Although these regions could not be used reliably by the stellar synthesis, their systematic residual structure can be exploited by a data-driven model, demonstrating that scientifically useful information remains concentrated in the parts of the spectrum that conventional analyses discard.

\textbf{Section Takeaway:} Residual spectra retain strong information about fitted global stellar parameters, more modest and partly indirect information about elemental abundances, and physically identifiable signatures of unfitted interstellar absorption and chromospheric activity. Cross-validation shows that these correlations are not always robustly predictive, particularly for the small activity sample. Most strikingly, the $6\,\mathrm{\%}$ of pixels excluded from the GALAH DR4 optimisation contain a disproportionate share of the remaining label-dependent structure.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The two complementary analyses presented above lead to a consistent conclusion: the mismatch between observed and best-fitting synthetic spectra is neither random nor devoid of information. The systematic audit identifies recurrent residual structure with plausible stellar, interstellar, and observational origins, while the data-driven experiments show that this structure remains predictive of both fitted and unfitted properties. At the same time, the abundance and activity experiments demonstrate that predictive correlations are not necessarily direct or causal. We therefore discuss first what the residuals reveal about the limitations of current spectral modelling, then what can and cannot be inferred from their predictive information, and finally how these results can guide progress towards modelling a larger fraction of the observed spectrum.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Residuals are systematic rather than noise}
\label{sec:discussion_systematic}

The mismatch between the GALAH spectra and their best-fitting synthesis is larger and more structured than expected from the reported observational uncertainties alone. Across approximately 99 million residual pixels, $4.6$ million exceed $3\sigma$, compared with approximately $0.3$ million expected for independent Gaussian noise. Correspondingly, only $63.4\,\mathrm{\%}$ and $95.3\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the residuals lie within $1\sigma$ and $3\sigma$, respectively, rather than the Gaussian expectations of $68.3\,\mathrm{\%}$ and $99.7\,\mathrm{\%}$.

Imperfect uncertainty estimates may contribute to this excess, but they cannot by themselves explain the strong wavelength dependence, recurrence, and preferred sign of many features. Numerous residuals occur at the same wavelengths across large fractions of the sample and are repeatedly either deeper or shallower than the synthesis. This behaviour points towards systematic differences between the observations and model rather than independent noise or isolated failures.

The sign of the residual provides useful first-order information. Features that are consistently deeper in the observations require additional or stronger modelled absorption, whereas features that are consistently shallower require less absorption or additional emission. The sign alone does not uniquely determine the cause, however. A deeper observed feature may result from an underestimated oscillator strength, a missing blend, inaccurate abundance, or an incomplete line profile. Similarly, a shallower feature may reflect excessive line opacity, line-core filling, or reduction and continuum-normalisation effects.

The residual field therefore contains several superposed components. Narrow recurrent features are frequently associated with stellar absorption lines, while the Balmer lines show broader, parameter-dependent core-and-wing structure. Interstellar absorption, residual sky emission, and broader detector- or reduction-related patterns contribute additional structure. Treating all of these residuals as a single additional noise term would obscure both their different origins and the information they retain.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{What the residual catalogue reveals about current spectral models}
\label{sec:discussion_residual_causes}

The classification of the 380 residual regions provides a direct overview of the origins of the recurrent signed structure. Figure~\ref{fig:residual_classification} places the adopted classifications on top of the fractions of spectra with significantly deeper or shallower observed flux. This presentation links the residual catalogue back to the original statistical audit and demonstrates that similar residual amplitudes and signs can arise from physically different causes. The category assignments therefore require the additional atlas, line-list, interstellar, and sky-emission diagnostics described in Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_peak_catalogue}.

\begin{figure*}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/percentage_3sigma_residual_flux_percentiles_m1_classified.pdf}
\caption{Classification map of the recurrent signed residual structure across the four GALAH wavelength bands. The underlying curves reproduce the fractions of \textit{baseline}-sample spectra for which the observed flux is more than $3\sigma$ shallower or deeper than the synthesis, as shown in the bottom panels of Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics}. Short coloured bars mark the 380 catalogue regions and are grouped vertically by their adopted primary classification: candidate line-strength and \textit{Line profile} mismatches in red and orange, \textit{Insignificant}, \textit{Skyline}, and \textit{Interstellar} regions in blue, and missing, unidentified, or ambiguous features in purple. The vertical offsets are categorical and carry no quantitative meaning; the horizontal bar widths are proportional to the fitted residual-region widths.}
\label{fig:residual_classification}
\end{figure*}

The catalogue is dominated by candidate stellar line-modelling issues. We classify 129 regions as \textit{$\log gf$ too high}, 94 as \textit{$\log gf$ too low}, and 13 as \textit{Line profile}. Together, these 236 regions account for $62\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the catalogue. Another 65 regions are either \textit{Known missing} (8), \textit{Unknown line} (38), or \textit{Unclear} (19). We identify 15 \textit{Skyline} and 12 \textit{Interstellar} regions, while 51 are classified as \textit{Insignificant}. Visual inspection confirmed the preliminary automatic classification for 312 regions ($82\,\mathrm{\%}$), while 68 classifications ($18\,\mathrm{\%}$) required revision.

These numbers show that inaccurate or incomplete stellar-line modelling is the largest identified contribution, but they should not be interpreted as a direct count of incorrect oscillator strengths. The classifications \textit{$\log gf$ too high} and \textit{$\log gf$ too low} describe the direction in which the total synthesised line strength must change. The underlying reason could instead be an unresolved blend, inaccurate abundance, incomplete hyperfine or isotopic structure, incorrect broadening or wavelength information, or limitations in the atmospheric and line-formation calculations.

\subsubsection{Line strengths, blends, and profiles}

Fe~{\sc i} contributes the largest absolute number of candidate line-strength mismatches, with 41 features classified as \textit{$\log gf$ too high} and 36 as \textit{$\log gf$ too low}. Substantial numbers are also associated with Ti~{\sc i}, Cr~{\sc i}, Si~{\sc i}, V~{\sc i}, Ca~{\sc i}, and Ce~{\sc ii}. These raw counts are descriptive rather than species-specific failure rates, because the number and strength of available transitions differ greatly between elements. Nevertheless, repeated mismatches identify groups of transitions for which the adopted line data warrant further investigation.

The most directly actionable cases are relatively isolated lines with a consistent residual sign. If a synthesised line is repeatedly too deep, its adopted opacity should be reduced; if it is too shallow, additional opacity is required. This does not uniquely identify which component of a blend is responsible, but it provides a well-defined direction for targeted line-list tests.

A particularly important example is the Si~{\sc i} line near $7800,\text{\AA}$, shown as the \textit{$\log gf$ too low} example in panel B of Fig.~\ref{fig:example_significant_residual_regions}. The synthesis systematically underpredicts the absorption associated with this feature. This matters beyond the Si line itself because it blends with the only Rb~{\sc i} line covered by GALAH. An underestimated Si contribution can consequently be absorbed into the inferred Rb abundance or otherwise destabilise the decomposition of the blend. Revising the Si~{\sc i} line strength is therefore a prerequisite for obtaining more reliable Rb measurements from this wavelength region.

The 13 \textit{Line profile} cases cannot be explained adequately through a scalar change in line strength. They include the Balmer lines and several asymmetric, split, or wing-dominated residuals associated with possible hyperfine structure, inaccurate component wavelengths, or imperfect broadening. Candidate examples involve Cu~{\sc i}, V~{\sc i}, Sc~{\sc i}, Ni~{\sc i}, Fe~{\sc i}, and Al~{\sc i}. These cases require improved component patterns or line-formation calculations rather than a simple adjustment of $\log gf$.

The Balmer residuals are the clearest large-scale example. Both H$\upalpha$ and H$\upbeta$ show broad residual structure whose amplitude and sign vary with stellar parameters, and both appear among the strongest activity-sensitive coefficients. Their behaviour could reflect several combined effects, including broadening, continuum normalisation, atmospheric structure, departures from LTE, and chromospheric filling. The current analysis establishes that their residual profiles vary systematically, but does not isolate the contribution of each physical mechanism.

Several residuals coincide with astrophysically important abundance diagnostics. The Li~{\sc i} feature near $6707.8,\text{\AA}$ shows a structured mismatch, while residuals are also present near important Al~{\sc i} lines. Broad CCD3 structure complicates the Eu~{\sc ii} region near $6645,\text{\AA}$, where local Co~{\sc i} and Fe~{\sc i} blends may additionally be imperfectly modelled. These examples show that broad instrumental or reduction structure can coexist with, and partly hide, local stellar-line problems.

The 8 known missing and 38 unidentified features provide a second set of priorities. The known missing transitions can be tested directly for inclusion in future line lists. The unidentified regions may contain omitted atomic or molecular transitions, poorly attributed blends, or residual reduction structure. Possible identifications involving rarely measured species remain especially interesting, but require confirmation from independent atlases, laboratory data, or spectra with different wavelength coverage and resolution.

\subsubsection{Non-stellar and reduction-related residuals}

The classification map also shows that not all recurrent residuals belong to the stellar synthesis. The 3 interstellar regions include established diffuse interstellar bands and interstellar K~{\sc i}. Their appearance in the stellar rest frame depends on the relative stellar and interstellar velocities, which can broaden the aggregate response or produce asymmetric residuals when an interstellar feature overlaps a fitted photospheric line.

The 15 skyline regions demonstrate an analogous effect for emission fixed in the observed frame. Imperfect sky subtraction leaves positive residual flux whose apparent position is shifted when the spectrum is transformed into the stellar rest frame. The strong activity-model response around $7798,\text{\AA}$ is associated with this skyline contamination rather than with the nearby Si~{\sc i}--Rb~{\sc i} blend. Its influence on Model~\textcolor{M4}{M4} illustrates how a small number of spectra can generate an apparent label correlation when observing conditions happen to correlate with the sampled activity distribution.

The broad undulating structure in CCD3 provides another example of a residual that cannot be addressed through individual stellar lines. It causes several local detections to be classified as insignificant and can complicate the interpretation of genuine lines embedded within the pattern. Its origin is more likely connected to reduction, normalisation, detector, or other broad instrumental effects than to a sequence of unrelated atomic transitions.

These cases motivate analysing residual components in their natural frames. Stellar-line residuals are most coherent in the stellar rest frame, interstellar features in an ISM rest frame, and sky or telluric features in the observed frame. A single-frame analysis necessarily broadens or blends some of these components and may conceal their true morphology.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Residual information is predictive, but not always directly attributable}
\label{sec:discussion_predictive_information}

The data-driven experiments establish that residual spectra contain predictive information, but also show that the physical interpretation of that information depends on the label, sample, and validation method. Tab.~\ref{tab:recovery_performance} summarises the recovery of the common global labels. Comparisons should be made primarily among models using the same sample: Models~\textcolor{M1}{M1}, \textcolor{M3}{M3}, \textcolor{M5}{M5}, and \textcolor{M6}{M6} use the \textit{baseline} sample; Models~\textcolor{M1}{M1*} and \textcolor{M2}{M2} use the \textit{abundance} sample; and Model~\textcolor{M4}{M4} uses the smaller \textit{activity} sample.

\subsubsection{Information about fitted stellar labels}

The strongest predictive result is the recovery of $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ from the residual spectra alone. These labels were already included in the GALAH optimisation, so their continued recoverability demonstrates that label-dependent structure remains after fitting. The mismatch is therefore not merely a set of constant line-list offsets, but changes coherently across stellar-parameter space.

Model~\textcolor{M1}{M1*} performs better than Model~\textcolor{M1}{M1} in both absolute and relative terms, showing that the residual information persists in the more restricted \textit{abundance} sample. Because this sample differs simultaneously in signal-to-noise ratio, quality selection, stellar-type coverage, and label distribution, the improvement cannot be assigned to a single cause.

The coefficient spectra show that this information is distributed across many wavelength regions. H$\upalpha$ and H$\upbeta$ provide strong $T_\mathrm{eff}$ and $\log g$ responses, while numerous narrow metal-line residuals also contribute. The $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ information is similarly distributed across many lines and blends rather than being dominated by one residual feature.

The abundance recovery is weaker and less directly attributable. Mg and Mn retain modest predictive information, while Na and Ba are recovered only marginally better than a central prediction. Moreover, the abundance coefficients are not dominated by residuals of the corresponding elements: they also contain Balmer-line, broad CCD3, and interstellar-associated structure. Model~\textcolor{M2}{M2} can therefore exploit chemical correlations within the training population rather than direct sensitivity to each abundance alone.

This distinction matters for transferability. A correlation that predicts an abundance within the GALAH training sample may not generalise to a population with different chemical or stellar-parameter correlations. Predictive recovery should therefore not automatically be interpreted as evidence that the residual model has identified missing opacity from the corresponding element.

\subsubsection{Information about unfitted and correlated properties}

The extinction experiment provides the clearest physical validation for an unfitted label. Although the strongly skewed $A_{K_\mathrm{S}}$ distribution limits the interpretation of the global recovery ratio, the more extinguished sightlines are preferentially assigned larger values. More importantly, the extinction coefficient recovers the strongest known diffuse interstellar bands and interstellar K~{\sc i}. Their independently known wavelengths and morphology demonstrate that the model identifies genuine interstellar absorption remaining outside the stellar synthesis.

The incomplete recovery of weaker DIBs is expected because the current analysis uses individual spectra in the stellar rest frame and is dominated by low-extinction sightlines. Relative stellar and interstellar velocities broaden weak features, whereas dedicated DIB analyses can shift spectra into an interstellar frame and combine many reddened observations.

The activity experiment provides a contrasting caution. H$\upalpha$ and H$\upbeta$ show plausible activity-sensitive responses, and several additional coefficient features coincide with lines previously associated with activity. However, the apparent in-sample recovery of $\log R'_\mathrm{HK}$ does not persist under cross-validation. The activity model also responds to residual skylines, especially around $7798,\text{\AA}$. The current sample therefore contains genuine chromospheric information, but it is too small and confounded for reliable out-of-sample prediction.

Together, the abundance, extinction, and activity experiments illustrate a hierarchy of interpretation. The extinction response is supported by independently identifiable physical features. The abundance responses are stable but partly indirect. The activity responses combine genuine stellar signatures with demonstrably unrelated observational correlations. Stability, prediction, and physical causality are therefore distinct properties that must be tested separately.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Towards modelling the full spectrum}
\label{sec:discussion_full_spectrum}

The comparison between pixels included in and excluded from the GALAH DR4 optimisation provides one of the clearest practical implications of this work. The 793 excluded pixels, representing approximately $6\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the available wavelength range, recover the global stellar parameters almost as well as the complete residual spectrum and better than the remaining 13134 optimisation pixels alone. The excluded regions therefore contain a disproportionate fraction of the remaining label-dependent structure.

This does not imply that these pixels should simply be returned to the original stellar fit. They were excluded because the synthesis or reduction was considered less reliable there, and the classification map shows why: these regions can contain inaccurate or missing stellar lines, line-profile mismatches, Balmer structure, interstellar absorption, sky emission, and broad reduction features. Allowing all of them to influence a conventional synthesis fit without additional components could bias rather than improve the inferred labels.

Instead, different classes require different corrective strategies. Relatively isolated line-strength mismatches can be addressed through revised oscillator strengths or additional blends. Structured profiles require improved hyperfine, isotopic, wavelength, broadening, or line-formation information. Interstellar features require a separate absorption component and velocity. Sky and telluric residuals require improved reduction or observational nuisance models rather than modifications to stellar opacity. Activity-sensitive regions require additional stellar labels and sufficiently representative training data.

A practical route forward is therefore to retain physical synthesis as the baseline while modelling well-validated residual corrections conditionally on stellar and observational labels. Such corrections should be restricted to wavelength regions where their behaviour is stable across independent stellar populations, fields, and observing conditions. Cross-validation within a single sample is necessary but not sufficient; transfer tests across nights or surveys would be needed to establish that the corrections are not survey-specific correlations.

Residual modelling could also help connect calculations of different physical fidelity. Sparse three-dimensional or non-LTE calculations might be used to learn targeted corrections to faster one-dimensional LTE synthesis where the residual behaviour is demonstrably coherent across label space. The present analysis does not establish such a mapping, but the recurrent and label-dependent line-profile residuals show where it may be most valuable.

The goal of using more of the spectrum should not mean forcing every pixel into a single stellar model. A complete description of an observed spectrum requires distinct stellar, interstellar, terrestrial, sky, instrumental, and reduction components. Progress towards modelling the full spectrum will come from identifying and separating these contributions rather than treating all remaining structure as stellar opacity.

%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%
\subsection{Limitations and priorities for future work}
\label{sec:discussion_limitations}

The present audit uses a restricted GALAH sample and therefore does not capture the full temporal and instrumental diversity of the survey. Residual structures associated with particular nights, fields, velocities, or reduction conditions may be over- or under-represented. Extending the analysis across independent observing periods would establish which features are stable throughout GALAH and which are specific to individual data sets.

The peak catalogue also depends on the adopted significance threshold, minimum width, and peak separation. These choices define a reproducible set of prominent residual regions, but not a unique decomposition of every mismatch. Broad structures can contain several local causes, while closely spaced blends may be represented by one catalogue entry. The classification map should therefore be interpreted as an overview of the dominant catalogue features rather than a complete partition of the residual spectrum.

The adopted classifications remain plausibility assessments. The residual sign often determines whether more or less modelled opacity is required, but does not uniquely distinguish oscillator strengths, abundances, blends, broadening, or atmospheric structure. The Solar and Arcturus atlases also represent only two regions of stellar-label space. Confirmation of individual line-data corrections will require targeted synthesis tests, additional benchmark stars, and laboratory information where available.

The data-driven models are limited by their training samples, label distributions, and quadratic form. Their performance describes interpolation within the sampled label space and should not be extrapolated to sparsely represented stars. Label covariance also permits indirect prediction, while small samples can produce strong correlations with individual observational artefacts, as demonstrated by the activity model.

Finally, the M5--M6 comparison is not a random-pixel experiment. The excluded pixels were selected specifically because they were less reliable for the original synthesis optimisation. The result therefore demonstrates that poorly modelled regions retain disproportionate information, not that an arbitrary set of 793 pixels would outperform the rest of the spectrum.

Immediate priorities include validating the highest-significance line-strength and line-profile candidates, correcting important blends such as the Si~{\sc i}--Rb~{\sc i} region near $7800,\text{\AA}$, separating stellar, interstellar, and observed-frame residual components, and constructing larger and more representative activity samples. Applying the same framework to independent surveys would further distinguish limitations common to stellar synthesis from those specific to the GALAH line list, instrument, or reduction.

The central implication is that residual spectra should not be discarded once a best-fitting model has been obtained. They provide both a diagnostic of where the physical and observational description remains incomplete and an empirical map of the information that has not yet been extracted.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

In this work, we introduced a stellar-rest-frame audit of the residuals between observed GALAH DR4 spectra and their best-fitting one-dimensional LTE and non-LTE synthetic spectra. We asked a simple question: after fitting a stellar spectrum, what information remains? If the synthesis, line data, stellar labels, data reduction, and uncertainty model provided a complete description of the observations, the residuals would be consistent with noise. Instead, across 7087 spectra from a representative three-night GALAH sample, we find coherent and recurrent residual structure throughout the four wavelength arms.

Our first result is a systematic map of this structure. Across approximately 99 million residual pixels, $4.6$ million exceed $3\sigma$, compared with approximately $0.3$ million expected for independent Gaussian noise. Many residuals recur at fixed stellar-rest-frame wavelengths and are preferentially either deeper or shallower than the synthesis, demonstrating that they cannot be explained solely through random noise or a uniform underestimate of the flux uncertainties. We quantified their amplitude, significance, sign, and recurrence as a function of wavelength in Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics}.

Using a reproducible peak-finding procedure, we identified 380 significant residual regions and classified them using their morphology and recurrence together with the Solar and Arcturus atlases, the GALAH DR4 line list, diffuse interstellar band catalogues, and sky-emission measurements. Candidate line-strength and line-profile mismatches account for 236 regions ($62\,\mathrm{\%}$), while the remaining catalogue includes known missing and unidentified lines, interstellar absorption, residual sky emission, insignificant detector-scale structure, and ambiguous cases. The classification map in Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_classification} shows that similar residual signs and amplitudes can arise from physically different causes, and that external diagnostics are required to distinguish them.

These classifications provide an actionable, rather than merely descriptive, audit of the spectral modelling. In many cases, the residual sign identifies whether the synthesised absorption must increase or decrease, although the underlying cause need not be the oscillator strength alone and may instead involve blends, abundances, wavelengths, hyperfine or isotopic structure, broadening, or line formation. Several regions affect important abundance diagnostics. One example is the underestimated Si~{\sc i} absorption near $7800,\text{\AA}$, which blends with the only Rb~{\sc i} line covered by GALAH and currently limits the reliable decomposition of that feature.

Our second result is that the residual spectra retain measurable astrophysical information. Models trained on residuals alone recover $T_\mathrm{eff}$ with robust scales of approximately $80$--$150\,\mathrm{K}$, $\log g$ with $0.14$--$0.19\,\mathrm{dex}$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ with $0.059$--$0.086\,\mathrm{dex}$, depending on the adopted sample. These labels had already been included in the original synthesis fit, so their continued recoverability shows that the remaining mismatch still varies coherently across stellar-parameter space.

The elemental-abundance results are weaker and more indirect. The residuals retain modest predictive information about Mg and Mn, while Na and Ba are recovered only marginally better than a prediction near the centre of their sampled distributions. Their coefficients are not dominated by clean residuals of the corresponding elements, but also respond to Balmer lines, broad wavelength structure, and interstellar features. Predictive abundance recovery therefore does not necessarily demonstrate direct sensitivity to missing opacity from that element; it can also exploit correlations among stellar parameters, populations, extinction, and abundance patterns.

For quantities not fitted by the synthesis, the extinction experiment provides the clearest physical validation. Although the strongly skewed $A_{K_\mathrm{S}}$ distribution limits the interpretation of a single global recovery metric, the more extinguished sightlines are preferentially assigned larger values. More importantly, the extinction coefficient independently recovers the strongest known diffuse interstellar bands and interstellar K~{\sc i}. The activity experiment provides a contrasting caution: H$\upalpha$ and H$\upbeta$ retain physically plausible chromospheric information, but $\log R'_\mathrm{HK}$ is not recovered reliably out of sample, and strong skyline residuals around $7798,\text{\AA}$ generate unrelated correlations in the small activity sample. These results demonstrate that predictive correlations, stable coefficients, and causal spectral sensitivity must be distinguished carefully.

A particularly striking result is that the 793 pixels excluded from the GALAH DR4 optimisation, corresponding to only approximately $6\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the available wavelength range, recover the global stellar parameters almost as well as the complete residual spectrum and better than the remaining 13134 optimisation pixels alone. These regions were excluded because the synthesis or reduction was considered less reliable there; their strong predictive performance shows that they contain a disproportionate fraction of the remaining label-dependent information. This does not imply that they should simply be returned to the original fit. Rather, their information can only be used reliably once their different stellar, interstellar, sky, and reduction-related contributions are modelled explicitly.

This result is particularly relevant in the current survey era. Large spectroscopic programmes provide sufficiently extensive observational samples to identify recurrent residual structure across stellar-label and observing-condition space, while modern synthesis grids and interpolation methods make large-scale one-dimensional LTE and non-LTE modelling increasingly tractable \citep{Xiang2019, Buder2025b, Storm2026}. A growing limitation is therefore not solely the volume of observations or the cost of producing synthetic spectra, but the fidelity of the adopted line data, line profiles, atmospheric physics, and observational model. Data-driven methods can identify and exploit correlations that physical synthesis does not capture, but these correlations must be validated through out-of-sample tests and independent spectral diagnostics before they can be interpreted as physical measurements rather than survey-specific coincidences.

The natural next step is to extend this audit to substantially larger and more diverse GALAH samples. This would allow residuals to be separated by stellar labels, fields, epochs, radial velocities, observing conditions, and instrumental configurations. Future analyses should also treat different components in their natural frames: stellar residuals in the stellar rest frame, interstellar absorption in an interstellar frame, and telluric and sky features in the observed frame \citep{Saydjari2023, Casey2026}. Observational nuisance labels such as airmass, humidity, sky brightness, and reduction diagnostics could then be modelled alongside astrophysical labels.

The residual catalogue can meanwhile guide targeted revisions of line strengths, blends, wavelengths, hyperfine and isotopic structure, and broadening information, as well as detailed comparisons with more realistic line-formation calculations. Sparse three-dimensional and non-LTE calculations may eventually be used to learn physically motivated corrections to faster one-dimensional synthesis in selected regions. The present analysis does not establish such mappings, but it identifies where the residual behaviour is sufficiently recurrent and label-dependent for them to be promising.

Our main conclusion is that residual spectra are not merely a quality-control product or discarded remainder of the fitting process. They reveal where the physical and observational description remains incomplete, which wavelength regions contain information that has not yet been extracted, and which apparent correlations require greater caution. Residual modelling is therefore not a replacement for physical spectral synthesis, but a means of diagnosing, validating, and prioritising its improvement.

In this sense, \textit{one spectroscopist's trash is another's treasure}: the mismatch left behind by today's best-fitting models maps the information that tomorrow's models should recover. Reading between the lines turns residuals from discarded imperfections into a route towards extracting more of the astrophysical information already present in every stellar spectrum.

\subsection{A catalogue of residual peaks and their plausible causes}
\label{sec:analysis_peak_catalogue}

Having established that significant residuals are wavelength-localised, recurrent, and often signed, we now turn the residual statistics into a reproducible catalogue of candidate residual features. The purpose of this catalogue is not to provide a unique decomposition of the full residual field, nor to assign a definitive physical origin to every feature. Instead, it provides a transparent set of significant residual regions that can be inspected, classified, and used to guide both the interpretation of the residuals and future improvements to the spectral modelling.

We identify residual peaks from the median residual-significance spectrum of the baseline sample. We use the median significance rather than the maximum significance so that the selection is driven by residuals that recur across many stars, rather than by individual outliers. The peak search is performed independently in each GALAH wavelength arm using the \textsc{find\_peaks} function of \textsc{scipy} \citep{Scipy}. As motivated in Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_significance}, we use the \input{tex_text/tuned_threshold}th percentile of the median residual-significance distribution in each arm as an arm-dependent reference level. We then require a minimum peak width of \input{tex_text/tuned_peak_width.tex} pixels and a minimum separation of \input{tex_text/tuned_peak_distance.tex} pixels. These choices suppress isolated single-pixel artefacts and avoid splitting broad residual structures into many closely spaced detections. The resulting peaks are shown as red dashed vertical lines in the middle panels of Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics}.

With this procedure, we identify \input{tex_text/number_significant_peaks_ccd1}, \input{tex_text/number_significant_peaks_ccd2}, \input{tex_text/number_significant_peaks_ccd3}, and \input{tex_text/number_significant_peaks_ccd4} residual peaks in CCDs 1-4, respectively, for a total of \input{tex_text/number_significant_peaks} candidate residual features. We emphasise that the exact number depends on the chosen threshold, width, and separation parameters. However, the goal is not to define a unique mathematical peak decomposition, but to construct a reproducible and interpretable catalogue of the most significant residual regions.

For the inspection of each residual peak, we combine several diagnostics in one figure. Several examples of these figures are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:example_significant_residual_regions} for residuals with different causes.

In the Fig.~\ref{fig:example_significant_residual_regions}, we use the residual morphology from Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics}: the peak logarithmic significance (yellow region), whether the residual is predominantly positive, predominantly negative, or mixed in sign (red and blue regions). Predominantly negative residuals, where the observations are deeper than the synthesis, point towards missing or underestimated opacity, omitted blends, or line-formation effects that make real absorption stronger than predicted. Predominantly positive residuals, where the observations are shallower than the synthesis, point towards overestimated opacity, lines that are too strong in the model, line-core filling, or reduction and continuum-normalisation effects. Mixed-sign residuals suggest either label-dependent modelling errors, imperfect line data that affect different stars differently, or observational effects that vary across spectra. Broad residuals, except for intrinsically broad stellar features such as the Balmer lines, are treated with particular caution because observed-frame telluric, sky, or reduction residuals can be broadened or smeared when spectra are shifted into the stellar rest frame.

In Fig.~\ref{fig:example_significant_residual_regions}, we also compare each residual peak to external spectral diagnostics. We use the Solar and Arcturus atlases of \citet{Hinkle2000}, together with their line identifications and telluric measurements, to assess whether a residual peak coincides with known stellar (black) or telluric (purple) absorption. We also compare the peaks to the GALAH DR4 line list (blue vertical lines), selecting atomic line entries with an estimated intrinsic central line depth of $\texttt{DEPTH} > 0.1$ for the actual line comparison and $\texttt{DEPTH} > 0.85$ for line visualisation \citep[see also Sec.~4.2 of][]{Heiter2021}. This selection is not a complete list of all possible contributors, but it captures many of the lines expected to be visible in high-quality Solar and Arcturus spectra. Finally, for features that are broad, mixed-sign, or located in known problematic wavelength regions, we compare against sky-line calibration observations (dark blue regions) where available.

We assigned each residual region a preliminary classification using a deterministic decision tree based on its wavelength, width, recurrence, residual direction, and proximity to features in the \citet{Hinkle2000} stellar atlas, the GALAH DR4 line list, the catalogue of diffuse interstellar bands (DIBs) by \citet{Vogrincic2023}, and the sky-emission spectrum. Broad features coincident with known DIBs or interstellar K~{\sc i} were classified as \textit{Interstellar}, while features coincident with sky emission were classified as \textit{Skyline}. Regions with significant residuals in fewer than $5\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the spectra were labelled \textit{Insignificant}.

For the remaining regions, we searched for stellar lines within one detector pixel of the residual peak. Where the \citet{Hinkle2000} atlas and GALAH DR4 identified the same transition, residuals that were predominantly shallower or deeper than the synthesis were classified as candidate \textit{$\log gf$ too high} or \textit{$\log gf$ too low}, respectively. Features present in the \citet{Hinkle2000} atlas but absent from the DR4 line list were classified as \textit{Known missing}, while features without a counterpart in either source were classified as \textit{Unknown line}. We then visually inspected all 380 regions. The automatic classification was confirmed for 312 regions ($82\,\mathrm{\%}$), while 68 classifications ($18\,\mathrm{\%}$) were revised. Most changes involved blended or mismatched line identifications, Balmer and hyperfine-structure residuals, the broader undulating pattern in CCD3, ambiguous skyline coincidences, or cases in which the residual morphology indicated a line-formation problem rather than a simple line-strength mismatch.

The final catalogue contains 236 peaks ($62\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the catalogue) for which we suspect issues with the line data, that is, either \textit{$\log gf$ too high} (129 peaks), \textit{$\log gf$ too low} (98 peaks), or \textit{Line formation} issues (13 peaks), for example because of wavelength, hyper-fine structure, or 3D non-LTE effects.
We believe that 51 peaks ($14\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the catalogue) are actually \textit{Insignificant}. For 24 peaks ($6\,\mathrm{\%}$ of the catalogue), we believe that either \textit{Skyline} (13 peaks) or \textit{Interstellar} (9 peaks) are the cause.
Most interesting are the 66 peaks ($17\,\mathrm{\%}$) of lines which remain an \textit{Unknown line} (38 peaks) to us, are \textit{Known missing} (8 peaks), or the specific reason for the residual is \textit{Unclear} (19 peaks) due to several possible causes.

Some additional numbers might be interesting: 
41 of the 129 peaks with \textit{$\log gf$ too high} were Fe~{\sc i} lines, followed by Si~{\sc i} (13), Ti~{\sc i} (11), Ce~{\sc ii} (11), Cr~{\sc i} (9), V~{\sc i} (8), Ca~{\sc i} (8), and Ni~{\sc i} (6). Of the ones with \textit{$\log gf$ too low}, 36 were Fe~{\sc i}, Ti~{\sc i} (10), Ce~{\sc ii} (9), and Cr~{\sc i} (5).

The known missing lines were Cr~{\sc i} (4), Fe~{\sc i} (3), and Si~{\sc i} (1).

Of the 19 unclear cases, 3 peaks were unclear for each of Mg~{\sc i}, Fe~{\sc i}, and Ni~{\sc i}.

For the line formation ones, it is of course the Balmer lines, but also several lines with potential issues regarding hyper-fine structure splitting (Sc~{\sc i} 5671.94, Sc~{\sc i}  5686.93, Ni~{\sc i} 7727.42, V~{\sc i} 5761.4198, Cu~{\sc i} 5700.2 or the wavelength for V~{\sc i} 5743.4466. discuss wavelength and HFS relative in relation to \citet{Jofre2017}!

We classified several features around 6650 as insignificant because of the underlying wavy pattern of CCD3 in this region. We do want to not though, that this also affected the Eu~{\sc ii} 6645 blend with potentially too high $\log gf$ values for Co~{\sc i} 6645.21 and Fe~{\sc i} 6645.3649.

These classes are intended as a plausibility ranking rather than a definitive identification. In several cases, the sign of the residual provides a direct indication of the direction in which the model would need to change. For example, a line that is consistently too shallow in the synthesis is classified as missing or underestimated stellar opacity. Such cases are particularly useful because they do not merely flag a problematic wavelength; they indicate the direction in which the line data should be revised.

A full version of Tab.~\ref{tab:tabular_peak_information} with extensive comments is provided as \href{https://github.com/svenbuder/residual_cannon/blob/main/data/peak_inspection_table.fits}{FITS file} in our public repository, together with all diagnostic figures of the \input{tex_text/number_significant_peaks} peaks in a repository \href{https://github.com/svenbuder/residual_cannon/blob/main/residual_inspection}{subdirectory}.

\textbf{Key Takeaway:} The residual peak catalogue shows that the significant GALAH residuals are not an unstructured collection of failures. Many can be associated with specific, actionable causes: missing or inaccurate stellar opacity, line-formation limitations, interstellar absorption, or observed-frame telluric, sky, and reduction residuals. The catalogue therefore turns the residual spectrum from a quality-control diagnostic into a prioritised list of spectral regions where improved line data, additional labels, or more realistic modelling are likely to recover currently unused information.
