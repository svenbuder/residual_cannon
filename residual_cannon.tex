\begin{abstract} % 250 words, 1920 characters
What if we could model 100\,\% of a stellar spectrum? Modern spectroscopic surveys measure stellar properties by comparing observed spectra to models, but even the best analyses leave residuals: wavelength regions where the data and model do not match. In a complete model, these residuals should look like random noise. Here, we show that they instead contain coherent information about stars, interstellar material, and observational effects. We use 7087 spectra from GALAH, a major Milky Way stellar survey, and audit the residuals between each observed spectrum and its best-fitting model. We quantify how large, significant, frequent, and directional these residuals are, and identify 254 significant residual regions across the observed wavelength range. By comparing these regions to spectral atlases, GALAH line information, telluric features, and sky diagnostics, we connect them to missing or inaccurate spectral lines, Balmer-line and activity-sensitive features, interstellar absorption, and reduction-related structure. We then train a data-driven model on the residual spectra themselves to ask what can still be learned after the best-fitting spectrum has already been subtracted. Residual-only models recover stellar temperature to $\sim75\,\mathrm{K}$ and iron abundance to $\sim0.05\,\mathrm{dex}$, and also retain information about abundance patterns, extinction, and chromospheric activity. Strikingly, even wavelength regions that were masked during the GALAH fit retain comparable information about stellar properties. These results show that spectral residuals are not merely imperfections to discard: they map the missing physics, missing labels, and observational effects that limit current models. Reading between the lines turns one spectroscopist's trash into another one's treasure, and provides a practical route toward extracting more of the information already present in stellar spectra.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

% Big picture: spectra encode stellar, Galactic, and planetary information.
Stellar spectra are among the most information-rich observables in astrophysics. Their absorption and emission features encode stellar atmospheric parameters, elemental abundances, line-formation physics, stellar activity, and non-stellar absorption along the line of sight. Large spectroscopic surveys use this information to reconstruct the chemical and dynamical history of galaxies, to trace the production and recycling of elements, and to connect stellar populations to the formation history of our Milky Way \citep[e.g.][]{FreemanBlandHawthorn2002, BlandHawthorn_Gerhard2016} and other galaxies \citep{VandeVen2025}. High-precision stellar spectra are also central to exoplanet science, where planetary radial-velocity signals have to be disentangled from stellar variability, photospheric line-profile changes, telluric contamination, and instrumental systematics \citep[e.g.][]{Fischer2016}. The scientific return from stellar spectra is therefore not set only by the number of observed stars or stellar populations, but also by how completely we can extract the information encoded in each observed photon.

% Hook: what if we could fit every pixel?
This raises a deliberately provocative question: what if we could fit 100\% of a stellar spectrum? In practice, every stellar spectroscopic analysis leaves residuals between the observed spectrum and the best-fitting model. These residuals are often treated as a quality-control diagnostic or as a nuisance to be masked, but they may also encode astrophysical, observational, and modelling information that the adopted spectral model has not captured. This is not only a conceptual issue: in the Galactic Archaeology with HERMES (GALAH) survey, one of the large-scale stellar spectroscopic surveys of the Milky Way, machine-learning interpolation made it computationally feasible to increase the fraction of the spectrum used for stellar-parameter and abundance fitting from typically $\sim20\%$ in Data Release 3 \citep[DR3,][]{Buder2021} to $87\%$ in Data Release 4 \citep[DR4,][]{Buder2025b}. However, even this modern synthesis could only use up to $94\%$ of the spectrum outside the typically neglected telluric O$_2$ Fraunhofer A-band region, because some observed spectral regions still could not be described reliably by the model \citep[][Sec.~4.3.2]{Buder2025b}. The central premise of this work is that the residual spectrum itself is a useful scientific object: it records where and how the model differs from the photons.

% State of the field: physical and data-driven models.
The modelling of stellar spectra has advanced substantially over the last decade \citep{Nissen2018, Jofre2019}. Modern survey pipelines \citep[see Tab.~1 of][]{Ness2026b} now increasingly infer many stellar labels simultaneously and across broad wavelength ranges, either through direct comparison to synthetic spectra or through flexible data-driven and emulator-based approaches \citep[e.g.][]{Ness2015, Casey2016, Ting2019}. Data-driven methods now play several roles in spectroscopy, including spectral generation, label inference, calibration, component separation, denoising, and residual analysis \citep[see][for a review]{Ness2026b}.

% Gap: models remain incomplete.
Despite this progress, no current survey analysis reproduces every observed pixel at the level implied by the observational uncertainties. Residuals can arise from several broad classes of effects: incomplete input physics, such as uncertain atomic and molecular data, missing blends, or incomplete hyperfine-structure treatment \citep{Heiter2021}; analysis choices, such as imperfect continuum normalisation \citep{Jofre2017}; fixed abundance-pattern assumptions, such as adopting solar-scaled abundance mixtures or fixed isotopic ratios despite their effect on line profiles \citep[e.g.][]{Coelho2007, Yong2004, Masseron2021}; missing stellar physics, such as limitations of 1-dimensional LTE or approximate non-LTE line formation, chromospheric activity, and spots \citep{Amarsi2018, Masseron2021, Lind2024, Lisogorskyi2019, Spina2020, Wilson2023b, Nordlander2024}; and non-stellar contaminants, such as interstellar absorption, telluric absorption, and sky subtraction residuals \citep{Saydjari2023, Casey2026}. This list is far from complete and does not even include one of the major unresolved complications in stellar spectroscopy: unresolved stellar multiplicity, where residuals from a single-star model can be further amplified by the unmodelled spectral contribution of one or more unresolved companions \citep{ElBadry2018, Merle2024}, which we acknowledge but bracket out of the present single-star residual audit. Regardless of their origin, positive and negative residuals provide more than a measure of fit quality: they provide an empirical map of the information that remains after the adopted model has been applied. An insightful example is the exploration of molecular C2 and CN features in the GALAH wavelength range. Originally not expected to be measureable as part of GALAH \citep{DeSilva2015}, probes of strongly carbon-enhanced stars \citep{Cotar2019b} proved fruitful for the rollout of C and N measurements for a significant portion of stars with less strong C2 and CN features \citep{Buder2025b}.

% From residuals as diagnostics to residuals as models.
Data-driven spectral models provide a natural way to test whether residuals contain structured information. \textsc{The Cannon} \citep{Ness2015, Casey2016}, as one of the most widely used data-driven fitting approaches, learns the dependence of continuum-normalised flux on stellar labels directly from a reference set, and its application to high-dimensional abundance space has shown that learned spectral derivatives can recover known line sensitivities and identify previously unrecognised abundance-sensitive features \citep[e.g.][]{Buder2018}. More broadly, \citet{Ness2026b} emphasised that residual analyses can uncover non-stellar signals and open a discovery space, while also stressing that data-driven models do not replace theoretical models. In this work, we apply this logic to residual spectra rather than to the observed spectra themselves. The goal is not to replace the physical synthesis, but to ask which label-dependent information remains after the synthesis has already been subtracted.

% Prior work and scope: residuals have been used before, but here for a stellar-rest-frame audit.
Residual and component-separation approaches have already proved powerful for non-stellar spectral structure. Data-driven methods have recovered extinction and diffuse interstellar band information from large stellar spectroscopic data sets, including in LAMOST and GALAH spectra \citep[e.g.][]{Ho2017, Buder2018, Vogrincic2023}. Recent component-separation models, such as \textsc{MADGICS}, explicitly separate stellar, dust, sky, instrumental, and residual components in large spectroscopic data sets \citep{Saydjari2023}. These studies demonstrate that residuals can contain astrophysically useful information from several distinct sources. Here, we deliberately take a complementary approach: we keep the residuals in the stellar rest frame and ask what a stellar-spectroscopy residual audit reveals about fitted stellar labels, abundance patterns, extinction, activity, and model limitations.

% Why GALAH and why this sample?
GALAH is particularly well suited to such an audit because it provides observed spectra, best-fitting synthetic spectra, flux uncertainties, stellar parameters, elemental abundances, extinction estimates, quality information, and residual-based diagnostic products for a large, homogeneous optical data set \citep{DeSilva2015, Buder2025b}. We focus first on a representative three-night GALAH sample. This keeps the analysis compact and transparent while still sampling ordinary survey targets across the broad GALAH magnitude range and 2-degree field of view \citep{Martell2017}, rather than selecting stars a posteriori by stellar labels or residual behaviour. A full survey-wide residual model is left to future work. We also use external activity indicators to test whether residuals encode spectral variability that is not part of the single-star photospheric synthesis.

% Paper structure and scientific questions.
We approach the residual spectrum in two steps with the data that we will introduce in Sec.~\ref{sec:data}. First, we perform a systematic audit of the residuals (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_audit}): we quantify their significance, sign, wavelength localisation, and recurrence across the GALAH wavelength range, and compare the strongest residual features to plausible stellar, interstellar, telluric, sky, instrumental, and line-list origins. Second (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon}), we use a data-driven model to test how much information can be extracted from the residual spectra alone. This separates the question of where the synthesis and the observations disagree from the question of what information those disagreements retain.

% Four questions that structure the paper.
Under the broader question of what information stellar spectrum residuals still contain, this work addresses four specific questions, which will later be discussed in Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,labelwidth=0em]
    \item How significant, systematic, and repeatable are the remaining residuals relative to the observational uncertainties, and how do they separate into over-predicted and under-predicted spectral features?    
    \item Which residual features can be associated with plausible stellar, interstellar, telluric, sky, instrumental, or line-list origins?
    \item To what extent do residuals alone retain information about stellar parameters and abundance patterns that were already included in the spectral-synthesis fit?
    \item To what extent do residuals retain information about quantities that were not directly fitted by the stellar synthesis model, but are known to influence observed spectra, such as extinction and stellar activity?
    % \item To what extent do residuals identify known physical or modelling limitations that could be addressed with improved line data, additional nuisance labels, or data-driven corrections, such as mappings between 1D LTE and 3D non-LTE spectra?
\end{enumerate}

These questions motivate residual modelling as a diagnostic rather than a replacement for physical synthesis. The aim is to identify where improved line data, additional nuisance labels, more realistic physical models, or data-driven corrections would most increase the information extracted from observed spectra.

\section{Data} \label{sec:data}

% Data overview.
This work requires three linked ingredients for each spectrum: the observed continuum-normalised flux, the corresponding best-fitting synthetic flux, and reference labels or diagnostics against which residual structure can be tested. Section~\ref{sec:data_galah} describes the GALAH DR4 data products used in this work, including observed spectra and uncertainties, best-fitting synthetic spectra, stellar parameters, elemental abundances, extinction estimates, and residual-based diagnostic quantities. Section~\ref{sec:data_samples} then defines the parent three-night GALAH sample and the derived baseline, abundance, and activity samples used for the residual audit and data-driven residual modelling.

\subsection{GALAH DR4 spectra and labels}
\label{sec:data_galah}

We use data products from GALAH DR4 \citep{Buder2025b}, based on spectra observed with the HERMES spectrograph \citep{Barden2010, Brzeski2011, Heijmans2012, Farrell2014, Sheinis2015} on the Anglo-Australian Telescope as part of the GALAH survey \citep{DeSilva2015}. HERMES records high-resolution optical spectra with a nominal resolution of 28000 in four non-contiguous wavelength channels, covering the blue ($4713$--$4903\,\text{\AA}$), green ($5648$--$5873\,\text{\AA}$), red ($6478$--$6737\,\text{\AA}$), and near-infrared ($7585$--$7887\,\text{\AA}$) arms for up to 400 fibres of the 2dF position system \citep{Lewis2002, Miszalski2006}. GALAH DR4 combines observations from the main GALAH survey with K2-HERMES, TESS-HERMES, cluster, halo, and other targeted HERMES programs. We return to the specific target selection of the representative sample in Section~\ref{sec:data_samples}.

The reduced one-dimensional spectra are produced with the HERMES reduction pipeline of \citet{Kos2017}, updated for GALAH DR4. This pipeline performs the extraction, wavelength calibration, sky subtraction, telluric correction, barycentric correction, uncertainty estimation, and line-spread-function measurement for each spectrum. For each spectrum, GALAH DR4 provides the observed flux, the associated flux uncertainty, and the best-fitting synthetic spectrum inferred by the DR4 analysis and used to normalise the observation, as exemplified in Fig.~\ref{fig:example_spectrum_170710003201361}, where we visualise residuals in three different intensities based on their significance with respect to the reported flux uncertainty.

In using these products, we retain the GALAH DR4 masking of the strongly telluric-contaminated region $7585$--$7680\,\text{\AA}$. A reliable treatment of this wavelength range requires dedicated telluric modelling alongside the stellar model, as demonstrated by \citet{Saydjari2023} and \citet{Casey2026}, whereas our aim is to assess residual structure in the DR4 analysis products rather than to introduce an additional telluric-modelling step.

The GALAH DR4 stellar labels were inferred from a hybrid spectrum-synthesis workflow. The synthetic spectra were computed with \textsc{Spectroscopy Made Easy} \citep[SME;][]{Valenti1996, Piskunov2017} and one-dimensional MARCS model atmospheres \citep{Gustafsson2008}, using the GALAH line list developed from the compilation of \citet{Heiter2021}. To make the analysis feasible across the full GALAH wavelength range, GALAH DR4 trains neural-network interpolators on synthetic spectra in local regions of $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ parameter space. These interpolators enable the simultaneous optimisation of global stellar parameters and elemental abundances across most of the observed wavelength range, including broad molecular C$_2$ and CN absorption features. The optimisation was done using up to 94\% of the pixels, with several pixels being excluded by default after initial tests indicated overall bad fit qualities for them \citep[for more details see Sec.~4.3.2 of][]{Buder2025b}. The 6\% of spectra that were synthesised but not used for optimisation are particularly interesting for this work and indicated as grey shaded regions in Fig.~\ref{fig:example_spectrum_170710003201361}.

The synthetic spectra include one-dimensional non-LTE line formation for atomic lines where available. In GALAH DR4, this treatment is implemented through departure-coefficient grids from the BALDER/Multi3D framework and associated model atoms for H, Li, C, N, O, Na, Mg, Al, Si, K, Ca, Mn, Fe, and Ba \citep[e.g.][]{Amarsi2020b, Amarsi2022}. Other atomic transitions and molecular features are treated in one-dimensional LTE. This distinction is important for the interpretation of residuals: although GALAH DR4 models the broad C$_2$ and CN absorption that affected earlier analyses, these molecular features do not yet have the same non-LTE treatment as the listed atomic transitions.

We use the GALAH DR4 labels as reference quantities against which residual structure can be tested. The main fitted stellar labels include the global stellar parameters $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$, microturbulence velocity $v_\mathrm{mic}$, projected rotational velocity $v\sin i$, and radial velocity $v_\mathrm{rad}$. We also use the reported elemental abundances for Li, C, N, O, Na, Mg, Al, Si, K, Ca, Sc, Ti, V, Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn, Rb, Sr, Y, Zr, Mo, Ru, Ba, La, Ce, Nd, Sm, and Eu. These are reported as $[\mathrm{X/Fe}]$ for most elements, with lithium reported as $A(\mathrm{Li})$. We use the associated abundance flags to distinguish reliable detections from upper limits, non-detections, and measurements affected by optimisation or boundary issues.

GALAH DR4 reports spectra on their native pixel grids, with individual wavelength solutions for each observed spectrum. Because the data-driven models used in this work require all spectra to be represented on a shared wavelength grid, we interpolate the observed fluxes, best-fitting synthetic fluxes, and flux uncertainties onto a common wavelength grid for each HERMES arm. The construction of the common grid for the representative sample is described in Section~\ref{sec:data_samples}.

We also make use of auxiliary quantities reported by GALAH DR4. These include extinction-related quantities such as $E(B-V)$ from the dust maps of \citet{Schlegel1998} and $A_\mathrm{K_S}$ estimated via the Rayleigh-Jeans Color Excess method \citep{Majewski2011}, as reported by \citet{Buder2025b}. These quantities are not stellar-atmosphere labels fitted by the spectral synthesis, but they provide useful reference labels for testing whether residual spectra contain information associated with line-of-sight dust and interstellar absorption. GALAH DR4 also reports Gaussian fits to residuals at the expected positions of interstellar K\,I and three diffuse interstellar bands \citep[see Sec.~6.1.3][]{Buder2025b}, as well as measurements of emission signals in the Balmer lines \citep[see Sec.~6.1.4][]{Buder2025b}. These are diagnostic products of the residual spectra and are not included in the stellar synthetic spectra used in the DR4 analysis.

\subsection{Parent sample and analysis samples}
\label{sec:data_samples}

% This paper is a first systematic audit, not yet a full survey-wide residual decomposition.
% Representative GALAH nights provides a compact, computationally manageable training set.
% There are other ways to create samples to probe residuals (e.g. from crossmatches with specific literature sources whose parameters such as logR'HK or X-ray luminosity one could probe for), but we will use one of the simplest approaches.

For this pilot study, we select spectra observed during three consecutive GALAH survey nights between 10 and 12 July 2017. This choice provides a compact but representative test set of ordinary GALAH targets, rather than a sample selected directly by stellar parameters, abundance quality, or residual behaviour. The selected fields are restricted to 22 pointings of nominal GALAH fields, where stars are selected randomly within a magnitude range
(3 bright fields with $9 < V~/~\mathrm{mag} < 12$, %  2532, 3897, 4592
18 main fields with $12 < V~/~\mathrm{mag} < 14$, % 67, 89, 178, 252, 285, 638, 689, 930, 982, 2120, 2831, 3459, 4286, 4559, 5436, 5792, 5971, 6415
and one faint field with $9 < V~/~\mathrm{mag} < 14.3$) % 6966
and therefore provide a useful first data set for testing whether systematic residual structure can be recovered across the typical stellar-parameter space of the survey. Across the three nights, 7627 observations of unique stars have been taken without repeated pointing at the same star.

In the following subsections, we describe how we create three separate samples as input for our residual analysis: a \textit{baseline} sample (Sec.~\ref{sec:sample_baseline}) with some quality cuts, an \textit{abundance} sample with additional requirements for quality and abundance measurements (Sec.~\ref{sec:sample_abundance}), and finally an \textit{activity} sample for which we select only sources with available activity indicators from a literature source (Sec.~\ref{sec:sample_activity}).

\subsubsection{Baseline Sample}
\label{sec:sample_baseline}

We filter these observations with several quality criteria. For each, the DR4 pipeline needs to have converged to a fit solution (\texttt{flag\_sp} $<2048$) with reasonable fit-quality of $\chi^2_\mathrm{sp} < 2$. This cut primarily removes spectra with strong molecular features and poor global fits, especially the coolest giants.
We further require finite values of both $E(B-V)$ and $A_\mathrm{K_S}$ and exposures in all four CCDs (\texttt{snr\_px\_ccd} > 10).
We do not remove spectra with emission-line signatures or binary flags, because such spectra provide relevant tests of whether non-stellar or poorly modelled spectral structure is recovered by the residual analysis.
These criteria leave a baseline sample of 7087 spectra with median signal-to-noise ratios of 29, 42, 55, and 50 in the four CCDs respectively. Each spectrum is from a unique star and no repeat observations are taken across the nights.

The distribution of this sample in the Kiel diagram is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:labels_m1_teff_logg_feh}. The sample covers the main stellar types observed by GALAH, spanning approximately $3600 < T_\mathrm{eff}/\mathrm{K} < 8000$, $0.5 < \log(g/\mathrm{cm\,s^{-2}}) < 5.0$, and $-3.0 < \mathrm{[Fe/H]} < 0.75$ (but mainly $-0.5 < \mathrm{[Fe/H]} < 0.1$). The vast majority of stars are observed at low extinction, $A_{K_S} = 0.02_{-0.02}^{+0.09}\,\mathrm{mag}$ up to a maximum of $A_{K_S} \leq 0.37\,\mathrm{mag}$.

For the representative sample, we construct a shared wavelength grid separately for each HERMES arm. We define each grid using the largest starting wavelength, the median wavelength dispersion, and the smallest final wavelength among the selected spectra. This gives common wavelength ranges of
\begin{align}
    4719.0299..&0.0460..4893.8808\,\text{\AA}, \notag \\
    5655.5298..&0.0547..5863.0182\,\text{\AA}, \notag \\
    6486.0104..&0.0631..6726.4572\,\text{\AA}\text{, and} \notag \\
    7689.1261..&0.0735..7874.3127\,\text{\AA} \notag
\end{align}
for the blue, green, red, and near-infrared arms, respectively. We interpolate the observed flux, best-fitting synthetic flux, and flux uncertainty onto these grids before constructing the residual matrix.

We define the residual spectrum as the difference between the observed and best-fitting synthetic flux,
\begin{equation}
    \Delta f_\lambda = f_{\lambda,\mathrm{obs}} - f_{\lambda,\mathrm{model}}, \label{eq:def_residuals}
\end{equation}
and use the reported flux uncertainties $\sigma_{f,\lambda}$ to calculate a residual significance,
\begin{equation}
    s_\lambda = \frac{\vert \Delta f_\lambda \vert}{\sigma_{f,\lambda}}, \label{eq:def_significance}
\end{equation}
and to construct inverse-variance weights,
\begin{equation}
    w_\lambda = \sigma_{f,\lambda}^{-2}. \label{eq:def_ivar}
\end{equation}
After requiring finite interpolated fluxes and uncertainties across the common wavelength grid, the final matrix contains 7087 spectra and 13927 wavelength pixels. This residual matrix forms the input to the data-driven models analysed in the later sections.

\subsubsection{Abundance Sample}
\label{sec:sample_abundance}

Based on chemical evolution studies \citep[e.g.][]{Kobayashi2020}, we know that the chemical enhancement of several elements is strongly correlated due to their similar nucleosynthetic origins. As a result, the absorption lines of these elements are also expected to be strongly correlated. We will use this insight and representative elements of a range of nucleosynthesis or element groups to search for residual lines who are correlated with these enrichment patterns. In particular, we use Mg to represent core-collapse supernovae-dominated elements, Mn to represent supernovae Ia-dominated elements, Na to represent the behaviour of several odd-Z elements, and Ba to represent asymptotic giant branch star-dominated neutron-capture elements. We only select spectra from the \textit{baseline} sample which also fulfil $\texttt{flag\_x\_fe} = 0$ for $\texttt{x} \in \mathrm{[Na, Mg, Mn, Ba]}$. As we are searching for possibly smaller signals, we increase our sensitivity to such signals by restricting the sample to slow rotators with typically narrow absorption lines, where most of the absorption signal is focussed in fewer pixels of our GALAH spectra. We thus further restrict this sample to slow-rotating stars ($\texttt{vsini} < 15\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$) and also cut a few dozen stars (less than 1\%) when enforcing $T_\mathrm{eff} < 6500\,\mathrm{K}$. We show the distribution of stellar parameters for the resulting \textit{activity} sample of 3826 spectra Fig.~\ref{fig:labels_m2_teff_logg_feh_xfe}. For further information, we also show the neglected hot stars and fast rotators as grey crosses and an inset of one of the abundance distributions, [Fe/H] vs. [Mg/Fe].

Ideally, we would want to also include the r-process enrichment pattern. However, Eu, as the sole element in the GALAH range known to be strongly dominated by this enrichment patter, is not well modelled in GALAH DR4. Similary, Sm, as the next most dominated element is not detected often enough. \section{A systematic audit of spectrum residuals}
\label{sec:analysis_audit}

Before asking whether residual spectra contain recoverable information, we first examine the residuals themselves. This section provides a systematic audit of where the observed GALAH DR4 spectra and their best-fitting synthetic spectra disagree, how coherent these disagreements are across the baseline sample, and whether their amplitude, sign, width, and wavelength location point to plausible astrophysical, observational, or modelling origins. The goal is not to assign a unique physical explanation to every residual feature, but to establish an empirical residual map and catalogue that are reproducible, interpretable, and useful for guiding the data-driven modelling in Section~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon}.

The audit proceeds in three steps. First, we quantify the amplitude, significance, sign, recurrence, and wavelength dependence of the residuals across the baseline sample (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_significance}). Second, we define a reproducible catalogue of significant residual features using a transparent peak-finding procedure applied independently in each GALAH wavelength arm (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_peak_catalogue}). Third, we compare these residual features to external line identifications, GALAH DR4 line-list information, telluric features, and sky-line diagnostics to associate them with plausible classes of origin (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_possible_causes}).

\subsection{Where do significant residuals occur?}
\label{sec:analysis_significance}

Two properties are central for interpreting a residual feature: its significance relative to the flux uncertainty, and its sign. If the observed spectrum shows stronger absorption than the model, $\Delta f_\lambda < 0$, the synthesis may lack absorption opacity, underestimate the strength of a blend, omit a relevant line, or misrepresent the line-formation physics. If the observed flux is higher than the synthesis, $\Delta f_\lambda > 0$, the model may over-predict absorption, miss chromospheric line-core filling, or absorb continuum-normalisation and reduction systematics into the fit. A signed residual analysis therefore separates under-predicted and over-predicted spectral structure, rather than treating all mismatch as a single unsigned noise term.

We expect 68\% and 99.7\% of residuals to be within $1\sigma$ and $3\sigma$ for a normal distribution. Of the 98 million pixels of our baseline sample, only 63.4\% and 95.3\% are within $1\sigma$ and $3\sigma$, respectively. So we certainly have systematic residuals, 2.4\% more $3\sigma$ outliers than expected (0.46 million instead of 0.30 million).

In Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics} (top panels), we are plotting the median residuals $\Delta f_\lambda$ per pixel (across the 7087 spectra) as a function of wavelength $\lambda$. What is interesting to see here? We see a lof of median residuals being close to 0. Of the 14 peaks below -0.1, we find 11 negative residuals in the bluest CCD1 (the strongest being 4748, 4771, and 4861Å), one at 5693.66 Å in CCD2, the Balmer Halpha line (6563Å), and 7800 Å in CCD4. We find less positive features, that is, 8, at 4745, 4792, 4888, 4891, 5668, 5754, 5831, and 7827Å. Beyond these peaks, we also notice some wave-like trend in CCD3 and some underlying smaller, yet broader residual behaviour in CCD1. There are also a lot of smaller residuals, who may not be as obvious in scale, but still significant relative to their uncertainty.

We hence turn to another way to judge residuals: their significance with respect to the noise. When calculating the median significance for each pixel across the baseline sample, we again find the vast majority of residuals being insignificant. As shown in the cumulative distribution of significance of Fig.~\ref{fig:cdf_significance_residual_flux_percentiles_m1}, 87.8\% of residuals are below $1\sigma$ on average, and 97.3\% below $2\sigma$, so only 2.7\% of residuals of a pixel are more significant than $2\sigma$ across the 7087 spectra. That is plotted on a logarithmic scale $\log s_\lambda$ as a function of wavelength $\lambda$ in Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics} (middle panels). In this figure we see a lot of peaks, indicating more significant outliers than when just using the amplitudes of residuals. We see that the underlying residuals patterns differ slightly, with the median significance being slightly higher for CCD3 and 1 than CCD2 and 3. This might reflect either the models being typically better for CCD2 and 4, or the uncertainties being systematically different for CCD1 and 3. In any case, it prompts us to restrict ourselves not to fixed significance thresholds across all CCDs, but rather look at outliers above the value of the 75th residual percentile of each CCD, that is significances above 0.92, 0.71, 0.87, 0.67 for CCDs 1-4 (indicated by dashed black horizontal lines in in Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics} (middle panels). We find a plethora of features that are above this region, and when coloring the pixels above the threshold in blue, we find not only a lot of individual residual peaks, but also find broader residual trends; of course around Halpha at 6563 Å, but also around 7715Å, for example. Before diving into each of these hundreds of peaks (and identifying them in a systematic manner in Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_peak_catalogue}), we are also interested in quantifying how often residuals tend to be positive or negative.

To do that, we calculate the percentage of the 7087 spectra, where the observed pixel flux is more than $3\sigma$ shallower or deeper than the synthesis and plot the percentage of significantly shallower observations in a pixel in blue and for significantly deeper observations in red in Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics} (bottom panels). Here, we only describe the patterns, with possible reasons being elaborated on in Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_possible_causes}. In general, we again see that most pixels seem to be modelled well, with low frequencies of significantly over- or under-predicted spectra. But when residuals appear, a lot of them seem to have a more specific direction. We find 9 peaks with significantly shallower observations for more than 50\% of spectra (4745, 4792, 4891, 5668, 5754, 5831, 6593, 6713, and 7827 Å), and 18 peaks 4731, 4740, 4741, 4748, 4756, 4765, 4771, 4784, 4805, 4809, 4861, 5694, 5754, 5757, 6502, 6563, 6718, and 7800 Å). The Balmer lines are almost exclusively significantly deeper than the synthesis. In addition, we find several regions in CCD4 which are broad and seem to appear with equally low yet noticeable frequencies to be significantly deeper or shallower. We also seem to identify the underlying wave-like pattern of residuals in CCD3, but most of this pattern stays below the $3\sigma$ threshold. It is, however, more prominent when looking at the behaviour of $2\sigma$ and $1\sigma$ shallower/deeper observations (appended in Fig.~\ref{fig:percentage_residuals_1_and_2_sigma}).

Several broad behaviours are apparent from Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics}. The strongest residuals occur at known high-information spectral features, including the Balmer lines. However, significant residuals are not confined to isolated stellar lines. Some residual structures extend over broad wavelength regions, while others are narrow enough to be associated with individual spectral features or small blends. The signed residual-frequency panel is particularly useful for distinguishing features that are systematically under-predicted by the synthesis from features where the residuals occur in both directions.

\subsection{A reproducible catalogue of residual peaks}
\label{sec:analysis_peak_catalogue}

To move from a visual inspection to a reproducible audit, we identify significant residual peaks from the median residual-significance spectrum of the baseline sample. We use the median significance rather than the maximum significance so that the selection is driven by residuals that recur across many stars, rather than by individual outliers. The peak search is performed independently in each GALAH wavelength arm.

We use the \textsc{find\_peaks} function of \textsc{scipy} \citep{Scipy}. In each arm, we first restrict the search to peaks above the 75th percentile of the median residual-significance distribution for that arm. We then require a minimum peak width of 4 pixels and a minimum separation of 15 pixels. These choices suppress isolated single-pixel artefacts and avoid splitting broad residual structures into several closely spaced detections. The resulting peaks are shown as red dashed vertical lines in Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics}.

With this procedure, we identify 71, 75, 72, and 36 residual peaks in CCDs 1--4, respectively, for a total of 254 candidate residual features. We emphasise that this catalogue is not intended to be a unique or complete decomposition of the residual field. Instead, it provides a reproducible set of significant residual regions for interpretation and comparison.

Before assigning possible causes to individual residuals, it is useful to group them by their empirical appearance:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Broad coherent residuals:} features extending over more than $\sim1$,\AA, often with structure superimposed on top. These may indicate continuum placement, blends, broad stellar features, diffuse interstellar absorption, telluric residuals, or sky-subtraction structure.
\item \textbf{Narrow line-like residuals:} features confined to a small wavelength interval and therefore plausibly associated with individual stellar lines or blends in the stellar rest frame.
\item \textbf{Signed residuals:} features where the observations are predominantly deeper or shallower than the synthesis, suggesting missing opacity, over-predicted opacity, or line-core filling.
\item \textbf{Mixed-sign residuals:} features with significant residuals in both directions, suggesting label-dependent modelling errors, imperfect line strengths, stellar variability, or observational effects that vary across spectra.
\end{itemize}

Known examples help anchor the classification. The Balmer lines produce some of the strongest residuals. Several broader features coincide with known interstellar absorption features, including the diffuse interstellar bands near 5780, 5797, and 6613,\AA, as well as the interstellar K~{\sc i} feature near 7699,\AA. In the infrared arm, several broader residual structures appear around 7750--7860,\AA and show up strongly in the signed residual-frequency panel, suggesting that sky subtraction or telluric residuals may contribute.

\subsection{Identifying plausible causes of residual peaks}
\label{sec:analysis_possible_causes}

\SB{This visualisation allows us to identify the typical character of a residual: almost entirely positive residuals suggest that a synthesised line was never visible or that we are looking at emission lines (which would not be modelled by the synthesis). Almost entirely negative lines would tell us about regions where the synthesis is misses the formation of absorption lines, for many possible reasons, one of the simplest being a line is not in the linelist. Lines with frequently both positive and negative residuals suggest inaccuracies in the linedata. Broader features (which the exception of the two broad Balmer lines in the GALAH wavelength range) would suggest a non-stellar contribution (with the shift to restframe blurring the feature).}

The purpose of this audit is to identify residual features that are common, significant, and potentially informative. Some features may motivate deeper follow-up analyses, for example by testing field-to-field variation, exposure-to-exposure sky-line residuals, or activity dependence. However, such investigations are not required for the main objective of this work. Here, they serve primarily to flag residual features whose origin is likely observational or astrophysical but not purely photospheric.

For each residual peak, we compare the GALAH residual statistics to several external diagnostics. We use the Solar and Arcturus atlases of \citet{Hinkle2000}, together with their line identifications and telluric measurements, to assess whether a residual peak coincides with known stellar or telluric absorption. We also compare the peaks to the GALAH DR4 line list, selecting entries with available oscillator-strength references and an estimated intrinsic central line depth of $\texttt{DEPTH} > 0.85$ \citep[see also Sec.~4.2 of][]{Heiter2021}. This does not provide a complete list of all possible contributors, but it identifies many lines expected to be visible in high-quality Solar and Arcturus spectra.

We use these comparisons to assign each residual peak to one or more broad classes:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Stellar line or blend:} the residual coincides with known stellar absorption in the Solar or Arcturus atlas and/or with a strong GALAH line-list entry.
\item \textbf{Potential missing or underestimated opacity:} the observed spectra are systematically deeper than the synthesis near a narrow stellar-rest-frame feature.
\item \textbf{Potential overestimated opacity:} the observed spectra are systematically shallower than the synthesis near a known modelled feature.
\item \textbf{Diffuse interstellar absorption:} the residual coincides with known interstellar features such as prominent diffuse interstellar bands or interstellar alkali lines.
\item \textbf{Telluric or sky residual:} the feature coincides with known telluric or sky-line structure, or appears as a broad/mixed-sign residual after transformation to the stellar rest frame.
\item \textbf{Balmer or activity-sensitive feature:} the residual occurs in a line core or region known to be sensitive to chromospheric activity, stellar variability, or imperfect broadening physics.
\item \textbf{Unclassified:} no plausible association is found, or multiple explanations remain possible.
\end{itemize}

\section{Modelling Residuals with \textsc{The Cannon}}
\label{sec:analysis_cannon}

Equipped with a detailed picture of potential causes of residuals, including several atomic absorption features as well as non-stellar features, we are now turning to the data-driven extraction of these information.

In this work, we use the data-driven code of \text{The Cannon}, which we briefly describe in Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon}. With this fitting code, we aim to extract correlations of residuals and labels that have actually been included in the fitting process with synthesis spectra (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon_fitted_labels}). We then move on extract correlations of residuals that have already been identified in prior work, such as interstellar medium residuals (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon_exinction_labels}), before trying to identify and validate correlations of residuals with labels that are informative (such as activity indicators), but not (yet) included or identified in GALAH analyses (Sec.~\ref{sec:analysis_cannon_activity_labels}).

\subsection{\textsc{The Cannon} - our data-driven analysis code}

We use \textsc{The Cannon} \citep{Ness2015}, in particular an updated version v0.9.0 maintained in a public repository\footnote{\url{https://github.com/ANU-RSAA/AnniesLasso} maintained by Marc White from ANU RSAA/AITC's Software Group.} of the code by \citet{Casey2016}.

For each flux residual $\Delta f_{n\lambda}$ of star $n$ at wavelength $\lambda$, we construct a data-driven model following the formalism of \textsc{The Cannon}. The model relates the residual flux to a label vector $\boldsymbol{\ell}_n$, which may contain stellar parameters, element abundances, or other quantities of interest,

\begin{equation}
    \Delta f_{n\lambda}
    =
    \boldsymbol{\theta}_\lambda^{\mathsf T}\boldsymbol{\ell}_n
    + \epsilon_{n\lambda},
\end{equation}

where $\boldsymbol{\theta}_\lambda$ is a wavelength-dependent coefficient vector and $\epsilon_{n\lambda}$ accounts for residual scatter. Throughout this work, we adopt a quadratic label model, such that $\boldsymbol{\ell}_n$ contains constant, linear, quadratic, and cross terms of the input labels. The coefficients $\boldsymbol{\theta}_\lambda$ then describe how the flux residuals vary with these terms at each wavelength.

To assess the reliability, we use both the recovery performance (Fig.~\ref{fig:recovery_comparison}), that is,
\begin{equation}
    \Delta \ell = \ell_\mathrm{Cannon} - \ell_\mathrm{DR4},
    \label{eq:label_residual}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Limitations of this first residual audit}
\label{sec:discussion_limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item The main analysis uses one representative night, not the full GALAH survey. Yet: one night provides a compact, natural GALAH sample;
    \item All residuals are analysed in the stellar rest frame.
    \item Telluric, sky, and interstellar features are therefore not modelled in their optimal rest frames.
    \item Residual-label correlations do not automatically imply causal physical interpretation; Yet: signed residuals and controlled label tests already expose systematic spectral regions;
    \item Label covariance remains a central challenge, especially for abundance groups, age/activity, and extinction.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

In this work, we have introduced a stellar-rest-frame residual audit of GALAH DR4 spectra. Starting from the observed spectra, best-fitting 1D (non-)LTE synthetic spectra, and flux uncertainties, we asked a simple question: after fitting a stellar spectrum, what information remains in the residuals? If the synthesis, reduction, line data, stellar labels, and uncertainty model were complete, the residuals would be consistent with noise. Instead, we find coherent, statistically significant residual structure across many wavelength regions in a representative three-night GALAH sample of 7087 spectra.

Our first result is a systematic map of these residuals. We quantified their amplitude, significance, sign, and recurrence as a function of wavelength, separating regions where the synthesis over-predicts and under-predicts the observed absorption (Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics}). Using a reproducible peak-finding procedure, we identified 254 significant residual regions across the four GALAH wavelength arms. By comparing these regions to the Solar and Arcturus atlases, GALAH DR4 line-list information, telluric features, and sky-line diagnostics (Fig.~\ref{fig:example_significant_residual_regions}), we associated residuals with a list of plausible families, including missing or inaccurate line data, Balmer-line and activity-sensitive residuals, interstellar absorption, and reduction- or sky-related features (Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_classification} and Tab.~\ref{tab:tabular_significant_peaks_classification}). This provides both a diagnostic of current spectral-modelling limitations and a practical blueprint for where future line-list and model improvements would have the largest impact.

Our second result is that the residuals are not merely systematic noise: they retain measurable astrophysical information. By applying \textsc{The Cannon} to residual spectra rather than to the observed spectra themselves, we tested how well stellar labels and external diagnostics can be inferred from the mismatch between the data and the best-fitting synthesis. Residual-only models recover $T_\mathrm{eff}$ to $\sim 75,\mathrm{K}$ and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ to $\sim0.05,\mathrm{dex}$ over the main label range of the sample, and retain weaker but measurable information about abundance patterns such as Mg, Na, Mn, and Ba (Fig.~\ref{fig:recovery_comparison}). They also recover information about quantities not directly fitted by the stellar synthesis, including $A_\mathrm{K_S}$ at the $\sim0.038,\mathrm{mag}$ level and the activity index $\log R'_\mathrm{HK}$ at the $\sim0.05$ level (Fig.~\ref{fig:recovery_comparison2}). These recoveries show that residuals encode not only shortcomings of the synthesis, but also stellar, interstellar, and observational information that is not fully captured by the current model.

A particularly striking result is that information remains even in wavelength regions that were not used in the GALAH DR4 optimisation. Models trained only on the optimisation pixels still recover $T_\mathrm{eff}$ and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$, as expected, but models trained only on the small fraction of synthesised yet masked pixels also recover these labels with comparable precision (Fig.~\ref{fig:recovery_comparison3}). This does not mean that the masked regions should simply be used without caution. Rather, it shows that some of the most problematic spectral regions are problematic precisely because they are information-rich: Balmer lines, poorly matched Fe lines, interstellar features, activity-sensitive regions, and reduction residuals all contain label-dependent structure that the current synthesis does not fully explain (Fig.~\ref{fig:cannon_linear_coefficients_model5}).

The natural next step is to extend this residual audit from the representative three-night sample analysed here to substantially larger GALAH samples, and ultimately to the full survey. A larger sample will allow residuals to be separated not only by stellar labels, but also by observing conditions, fields, epochs, and instrumental configurations. Future models should also treat different residual components in their appropriate rest frames: stellar residuals in the stellar rest frame, interstellar features in the interstellar rest frame, and telluric, sky, and airglow residuals in the observed frame \citep{Saydjari2023, Casey2026}. Such models could include observational nuisance labels, such as airmass, humidity, sky brightness, or reduction diagnostics, alongside astrophysical labels. At the same time, the residual catalogue presented here can be used to prioritise targeted line-list revisions, re-synthesis with tools such as \textsc{SME} or \textsc{Korg}, direct comparisons to 3D non-LTE predictions for features such as the Balmer lines \citep[e.g.][]{Amarsi2018}, and tests of whether activity-sensitive residuals can become practical diagnostics in spectral regions without Ca~H\&K coverage. More broadly, data-driven residual models may provide an efficient way to map computationally cheaper 1D LTE or non-LTE spectra onto the behaviour expected from more realistic but expensive 3D non-LTE calculations.

Our main conclusion is therefore that residual spectra are not merely a quality-control product, but a diagnostic of what current spectral models do not yet explain. They reveal where line data are incomplete, where 1D LTE or non-LTE approximations break down, where additional labels such as activity, extinction, or observing conditions are needed, and where reduction or calibration residuals still imprint themselves on survey spectra. Residual modelling is therefore not a replacement for physical spectral synthesis, but a way to prioritise its improvement.

In this sense, \textit{one spectroscopist's trash is another one's treasure}: the mismatch left behind by today's best-fitting models maps the information that tomorrow's models should recover. Reading between the lines turns residuals from discarded imperfections into a route toward extracting more of the astrophysical information already present in every stellar spectrum.

When only using the 94 percent of the wavelength range that were used for GALAH DR4, we still recover Teff within 190K and [Fe/H] within 0.11dex. Somewhat surprisingly, when only using the 6 percent of the wavelength range that were not used for the fit (but synthesised), we recover Teff within 160K and [Fe/H] within 0.10dex  (Fig.~\ref{fig:recovery_comparison2})! Looking at the coefficients  (Fig.~\ref{fig:cannon_linear_coefficients_model5}), that is because the Balmer lines and several badly matched Fe lines include considerable information on Teff, logg and [Fe/H]! So long story short, the take away: Take-away A: After a systematic residual audit: A LOT! => Blueprint for model improvements! Take-away B: Residuals clearly include information on: systematic shortcomings in stellar models (e.g. 3D NLTE), nuanced, ignored, hard-to-model stellar properties (activity), calibration residuals (positive and negative residuals, e.g. skylines), non-stellar effects (e.g. activity!) We can use data-driven models to extract them!

%%% First idea of the introduction:

A first motivation is "What if we could model 100% of a spectrum?" While this is a reference to a meme, it opens up a specific avenue of investigation: What effects are currently preventing us from using 100% of the information that observations give us (that is, flux and flux uncertainty as a function of wavelength)? In particular, based on my analysis of GALAH DR4, where I have traced the percentage of pixels used for the synthesis, only up to 94% of the pixels can be used for optimisation (others I had to exclude because of usual bad fits as described out in \citet[][Sec.~4.3.2]{Buder2025b}).

Being able to fit spectra well is important for a lot of domains: it is timely for Gaia DR4, which will deliver time-domain spectroscopy for millions of stars. It is important for planet hunters (or hunters of any non-stellar signal that is potentially dwarfed by stellar effects). Similarly we have not identified all lines in spectra and we cannot model everything realistic (e.g. chromosphere or other effects influencing Balmer lines -> Amarsi) > Galah DR4 only able to include only up to 94%! What if some signatures (e.g. of rare elements) hidden? Only recently extracted C and N abundances from GALAH spectra based on proof-of-concept from CEMP stars (Cotar) -> aim to use Cannon to see which nucleosynthesis group residuals may correlate with. And of course extinction! -> exciting potential to let spectra tell us about this effect that significantly effects photometry (important beyond Gaiasphere with precise enough parallax measurements). Not to forgot potential to predict 3D non-LTE effects (or even corrections?) Potential: train Cannon (or any spectrum interpolator) to map between cheap 1D LTE and 3D non-LTE instead of having to calculate more expensive 1D non-LTE or even more expensive 3D non-LTE models for each spectrum (it will still require the training set to be done in 3D non-LTE, but then the 1D LTE -> 3D non-LTE mapping could approached as a data-driven mapping rather than an astrophysical numerical one)!

Here are a few ideas (already pointing into a direction that I believe we could use a data-driven approach to tell us which parts of a spectrum are susceptible to these influences within the GALAH wavelength range):
- 3D non-LTE effects, e.g. \citep[][]{Amarsi2018} http://adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2018A%26A...615A.139A (currently we are only modelling 1D LTE or 1D non-LTE at best) -> all lines, but particularly the two Balmer lines of Halpha and Hbeta
- missing line data (e.g. a specific line is not in the line list of GALAH which is heavily based of the Gaia ESO linelist by \citet{Heiter2021} https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2021A&A...645A.106H and thus is not in the model) -> this is possibly the case for ~20 lines 
- lines that are strongly affected by stellar activity (while the CaHK lines are not in the GALAH wavelength range, \citet{Spina2020} https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2020ApJ...895...52S showed for example a strong change in lines such as the Ba II line at 5853Å and how stellar activity significantly effects measurements above log R'HK of -4.6, but even already starting from -5.0. I am able to create a training set 2 of 228 GALAH dwarf stars and spectra that has log R'HK information from \citet{Zhang2024} https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2024A&A...688A..23Z to probe which lines are most sensitive to this label. There are some previous works (with different approaches) that aimed to identify activity sensitive lines, such as \citet{Wise2018} https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2018AJ....156..180W who found 4827.46, 4861.33, 5707.00, 6562.81. but also work by \citet{Lisogorskyi2019} https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2019MNRAS.485.4804L, with lines within the GALAH wavelength range.
- Stellar spots (somewhat overlapping with activity) can change measurements, with approaches to model this tested by \citet{Wilson2023b} https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2023MNRAS.524..731W and \citet{Nordlander2024} https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2024MNRAS.tmp.2465N. -> such additional models would maybe not be necessary, if \textsc{The Cannon} could already include activity influences intrinsically.

I already tested a first idea on how to approach the extraction of signals with The Cannon:
- calculate the residuals between observed and best-fit spectra for a random night of GALAH DR4 (one that samples the HRD with the typical survey setup). One might have to at least take a bit more care of cutting wrong features, such as the occasional hot pixel causing a flux above 1.X.
- Use them as input (rather than the observed flux) for \textsc{The Cannon} to see how well we can recover Teff, logg, and [Fe/H] - aka: how much information is left in the data? Surprise: We still get Teff correct to 60 - 230 + 360 K, logg to 0.05 - 0.25 + 0.42 and [Fe/H] to 0.02 -0.11 + 0.09. (After a cleaning step that gets rid of the usual few outliers with bad spectra, I even seem to get -10 -160+23- for Teff, -0.01 - 0.20 + 0.21 for logg, and 0.00 - 0.11+0.09 for [Fe/H]). This is mindblowing, given that this is only twice as bad for [Fe/H] than the results that use 94% of the spectrum (possibly a few very informative lines have been omitted or not included in the DR4 analysis or are not well fitted by the 1D non-LTE analysis, yet highly informative?). For Teff and logg it is also still a strong indication, that teff- and logg-dependent information remains left in the residuals / is not covered by the synthesis (e.g. Teff/logg-dependent 3D non-LTE effects that may differ across the HRD).
- I could imagine going through the specific linear coefficients of \textsc{The Cannon} to systematically identify the residual information (e.g. I clearly see the Halpha line pop up as negatively corellated in the Teff coefficient, but several smaller features show up as positively correlated. For [Fe/H], there are a lot of positively correlated features (I assume lines that have not been included in the synthesis, but of course are expected to scale with iron abundance or more specifically overall metallicity).
- instead of just tracing metallicity / [Fe/H], I was actually thinking of tracing more specific element groups, that is Mg (for CCSNe channel), Mn (for SNIa), the mean of Na and Al (for odd-Z), and the mean of Ba and Y (for s-process), possibly even Nd or Na+Eu (for r-process). That way, I could make a much more specific assessment of how important a specific residual is (as odd-Z and s/r-process are exciting, but CCSne and SNIa not necessarily). I could also imagine that specific lines may already be known and just simply have unreliable linedata (so I could help to make predictions as to which linedata may need improvement).

I am wondering, what would be the most systematic approach: Looking into the remaining residuals of the residuals Cannon - to see what information is not yet covered? That way, I could definitely identify the interstellar features (this has already been proven to work by \citet{Ho2017} http://adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2017ApJ...836....5H with the extinction label A\_Ks to identify DIBs 4428, 5780, 5797, and 6283, as well as myself for GALAH DR2 \citep{Buder2018} http://adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2018MNRAS.478.4513B for DIBs around 5700, 5780, 5797, 6613, and the interstellar KI line around 7699). When adding E(B-V), I can reproduce that estimate within -0.01 - 0.09+0.10 mag, and similarly, I can reproduce A\_Ks within -0.0002 - 0.033+0.046 mag. Then the next step could be to see which residuals correlate with specific element groups / nucleosynthesis channels to get a better idea / prediction of their source (this would require more careful comparison with literature).

When going to the training set and model 2 that also has log R'HK in it (only for dwarfs), I am able to recover stellar parameters even better (e.g. Teff within 5-78+89 K, but also logR'HK within 0.003-0.067+0.042. Using A\_Ks instead of E(B-V) for that model leads to a recovery of very similar recovery of log R'HK of 0.001-0.065+0.046. In both cases, \textsc{The Cannon} identifies a few lines as sensitive to logR'HK: correlating positively are lines around 6508+-5, 6518+-5, 6700+-5, 7780+-5, 7835+-5 (I can extract them more than that). There are also a few features that anti-correlate with this label, such as strongly the core of the Balmer line Halpha and at 7800 (this line has never been fitted well in most of GALAH and is not excluded in DR4 because it often did not match well), as well as a feature at 7855+-5. Some of these may not be actually be lines but just individual pixels -- but I can confirm that and clean the list to only involve actual lines. I could compare the linear coefficient with the lines from \citet{Wise2018} and \citet{Lisogorskyi2019}. I have to do that more carefully, but I think FeI at 4772.82, NiI at 6643.64 might match.

When presenting the coefficients of \textsc{The Cannon} and identifying significant features, I might want to use the scipy find\_peak algorithm to set a reasonable limit on the width (aka avoid 1 pixel) and the amplitude (e.g. be significantly different from the noise), to automate the process.

I was also wondering about a good way to log my progress, e.g.: start by saying how significant (with respect to the observational uncertainty) the mismatch between observation and synthesis is. That might be good to quantify with a few numbers: a) what is the average flux residual in normalised flux, b) what is the typical noise in the same unit, c) what is the typical significance of the residuals, and how many pixels and percent of the 16000 disagree by more than 1sigma, 2sigma, 3sigma (aka how much discovery space is there)?

And even different types of data-driven tellurics (e.g. O2, H2O etc.) and skyline estimates for the non-stellar non-interstellar layer. and the different dibs with an RV signal for the interstellar layer.
