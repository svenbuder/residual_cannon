\begin{abstract}
What if we could fit 100 percent of a stellar spectrum?
Modern spectroscopic surveys infer stellar parameters and chemical abundances by comparing observed spectra to synthetic models, yet even state-of-the-art 1D LTE/NLTE analyses leave structured residuals behind.
In this work, we treat these residuals not as nuisance noise, but as a diagnostic of the information that current spectral models do not yet capture.
Using a representative one-night sample from the GALAH survey, we compare observed spectra to their best-fitting synthetic spectra and analyse the resulting signed residuals, separating over-predicted and under-predicted spectral features.
We first quantify the significance of the residuals relative to the observational uncertainties.
We then use data-driven models to test how much information about fitted stellar labels, abundance patterns, extinction, and stellar activity remains in the residual spectra alone.
Finally, we identify residual features associated with known physical and modelling limitations, including missing or inaccurate line data, activity-sensitive lines, and possible 1D LTE/NLTE modelling shortcomings.
This systematic residual audit provides a practical route to prioritising spectral regions where improved line lists, additional nuisance labels, or data-driven corrections to physical models would most increase the information extracted from stellar spectra.

% What if we could model 100\% of a spectrum? Here, we investigate the diagnostic power of spectral residuals - the difference between observed and best-fit model stellar spectra - as a source of information on stellar and interstellar phenomena not captured by current best-fit spectral syntheses. By fitting a data-driven model to these residuals, we demonstrate that fundamental stellar parameters can be recovered to surprising precision ($\Delta T_\mathrm{eff} < 350\,\mathrm{K}$, $\Delta \log g < 0.4$, and $\Delta\mathrm{[Fe/H]} < 0.1$), indicating that significant parameter-dependent structure remains in the residuals. This unmodelled information likely includes physical effects such as 3-dimensional atmosphere effects (not covered by 1-dimensional synthesis), stellar activity (e.g. linked to X-ray luminosity or indices like $\log R_{HK}$), and interstellar absorption features like diffuse interstellar bands (DIBs) and KI lines.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

% Big picture: spectra encode stellar, Galactic, and planetary information.
Stellar spectra are among the most information-rich observables in astrophysics. Their absorption and emission features encode stellar atmospheric parameters, elemental abundances, line-formation physics, stellar activity, and non-stellar absorption along the line of sight. Large spectroscopic surveys use this information to reconstruct the chemical and dynamical history of galaxies, to trace the production and recycling of elements, and to connect stellar populations to the formation history of our Milky Way \citep[e.g.][]{FreemanBlandHawthorn2002, BlandHawthorn_Gerhard2016} and other galaxies \citep{VandeVen2025}. High-precision stellar spectra are also central to exoplanet science, where planetary radial-velocity signals have to be disentangled from stellar variability, photospheric line-profile changes, telluric contamination, and instrumental systematics \citep[e.g.][]{Fischer2016}. The scientific return from stellar spectra is therefore not set only by the number of observed stars or stellar populations, but also by how completely we can extract the information encoded in each observed photon.

% Hook: what if we could fit every pixel?
This raises a deliberately provocative question: what if we could fit 100\% of a stellar spectrum? In practice, every stellar spectroscopic analysis leaves residuals between the observed spectrum and the best-fitting model. These residuals are often treated as a quality-control diagnostic or as a nuisance to be masked, but they may also encode astrophysical, observational, and modelling information that the adopted spectral model has not captured. This is not only a conceptual issue: in the Galactic Archaeology with HERMES (GALAH) survey, one of the large-scale stellar spectroscopic surveys of the Milky Way, machine-learning interpolation made it computationally feasible to increase the fraction of the spectrum used for stellar-parameter and abundance fitting from typically $\sim20\%$ in Data Release 3 \citep[DR3][]{Buder2021} to $87\%$ in Data Release 4 \citep[DR4][]{Buder2025b}. However, even this modern synthesis could only use up to $94\%$ of the spectrum outside the typically neglected telluric O$_2$ Fraunhofer A-band region, because some observed spectral regions still could not be described reliably by the model \citep[][Sec.~4.3.2]{Buder2025b}. The central premise of this work is that the residual spectrum itself is a useful scientific object: it records where and how the model differs from the photons.

% State of the field: physical and data-driven models.
The modelling of stellar spectra has advanced substantially over the last decade \citep{Nissen2018, Jofre2019}. Modern survey pipelines \citep[see Tab.~1 of][]{Ness2026b} now increasingly infer many stellar labels simultaneously and across broad wavelength ranges, either through direct comparison to synthetic spectra or through flexible data-driven and emulator-based approaches \citep[e.g.][]{Ness2015, Casey2016, Ting2019}. Data-driven methods now play several roles in spectroscopy, including spectral generation, label inference, calibration, component separation, denoising, and residual analysis \citep[see][for a review]{Ness2026b}.

% Gap: models remain incomplete.
Despite this progress, no current survey analysis reproduces every observed pixel at the level implied by the observational uncertainties. Residuals can arise from several broad classes of effects: incomplete input physics, such as uncertain atomic and molecular data, missing blends, or incomplete hyperfine-structure treatment \citep{Heiter2021}; analysis choices, such as imperfect continuum normalisation \citep{Jofre2017}; fixed abundance-pattern assumptions, such as adopting solar-scaled abundance mixtures or fixed isotopic ratios despite their effect on line profiles \citep[e.g.][]{Coelho2007, Yong2004, Masseron2021}; missing stellar physics, such as limitations of 1-dimensional LTE or approximate non-LTE line formation, chromospheric activity, and spots \citep{Amarsi2018, Masseron2021, Lind2024, Lisogorskyi2019, Spina2020, Wilson2023b, Nordlander2024}; and non-stellar contaminants, such as interstellar absorption, telluric absorption, and sky subtraction residuals \citep{Saydjari2023, Casey2026}. This list is far from complete and does not even include one of the major unresolved complications in stellar spectroscopy: unresolved stellar multiplicity, where residuals from a single-star model can be further amplified by the unmodelled spectral contribution of one or more unresolved companions \citep{ElBadry2018, Merle2024}, which we acknowledge but bracket out of the present single-star residual audit. Regardless of their origin, positive and negative residuals provide more than a measure of fit quality: they provide an empirical map of the information that remains after the adopted model has been applied. An insightful example is the exploration of molecular C2 and CN features in the GALAH wavelength range. Originally not expected to be measureable as part of GALAH \citep{DeSilva2015}, probes of strongly carbon-enhanced stars \citep{Cotar2019b} proved fruitful for the rollout of C and N measurements for a significant portion of stars with less strong C2 and CN features \citep{Buder2025b}.

% Method idea: apply data-driven models to residuals.
Data-driven spectral models provide a natural way to test whether residuals contain structured information. \textit{The Cannon} \citep{Ness2015, Casey2016}, as one of the most widely used data-driven fitting approaches, learns the dependence of continuum-normalised flux on stellar labels directly from a reference set, and its application to high-dimensional abundance space showed that learned spectral derivatives can recover known line sensitivities and identify previously unrecognised abundance-sensitive features \citep[e.g.][]{Buder2018}. More broadly, \citet{Ness2026b} emphasised that residual analyses can uncover non-stellar signals and open a discovery space, while also stressing that data-driven models do not replace theoretical models. In this work, we apply this logic to residual spectra rather than to the observed spectra themselves. The goal is not to replace the physical synthesis, but to ask which label-dependent information remains after the synthesis has already been subtracted.

% Prior work and scope: residuals have been used before, but here for a stellar-rest-frame audit.
Residual and component-separation approaches have already proved powerful for non-stellar spectral structure. Data-driven methods have recovered extinction and diffuse interstellar band information from large stellar spectroscopic data sets, including in LAMOST and GALAH spectra \citep[e.g.][]{Ho2017, Buder2018, Vogrincic2023}. Recent component-separation models, such as \textsc{MADGICS}, explicitly separate stellar, dust, sky, instrumental, and residual components in large spectroscopic data sets \citep{Saydjari2023}. These studies demonstrate that residuals can contain astrophysically useful information of different classes of sources. As we aim to audit residuals with an empirical data-driven approach, we deliberately keep the residuals in the stellar rest frame and ask what a stellar-spectroscopy residual audit reveals about fitted labels, abundance patterns, extinction, activity, and model limitations.

% Why GALAH and why one night?
GALAH is particularly well suited to such an audit because it provides observed spectra, best-fitting synthetic spectra, flux uncertainties, stellar parameters, elemental abundances, extinction estimates, and quality information for a large, homogeneous optical data set \citep{DeSilva2015, Buder2025b}. We focus first on a representative GALAH observing night, which keeps the training set compact and transparent while still sampling ordinary survey targets within the broad GALAH magnitude range and 2-degree field of view \citep{Martell2017}, rather than through a posteriori cuts on stellar labels or residual behaviour. A second night provides a validation set for the strongest residual features, while a full survey-wide residual model is left to future work. We also use external activity indicators to test whether residuals encode spectral variability that is not part of the single-star photospheric synthesis.

% Four questions that structure the paper.
Under the broader question of what information stellar spectrum residuals still contain, this work addresses four specific questions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em,labelwidth=0em]
    \item How significant and systematic are the remaining residuals relative to the observational uncertainties, and how do these residuals separate into over-predicted and under-predicted spectral features?

    \item To what extent do residuals alone retain information about stellar parameters and abundance patterns that were already included in the spectral-synthesis fit?

    \item To what extent do residuals retain information about quantities that were not directly fitted by the stellar synthesis model, but are known to influence observed spectra, such as extinction and stellar activity?

    \item To what extent do residuals identify known physical or modelling limitations that could be addressed with improved line data, additional nuisance labels, or data-driven corrections, such as mappings between 1D LTE and 3D non-LTE spectra?
\end{enumerate}

These questions motivate data-driven residual modelling as a diagnostic rather than a replacement for physical synthesis: a way to identify where improved line data, additional nuisance labels, or more realistic physical models would most increase the information extracted from observed spectra.

\section{Data} \label{sec:data}

% Data overview.
This work requires three linked ingredients for each spectrum: the observed normalised flux, the corresponding best-fitting synthetic flux, and labels against which residual structure can be tested.
Section~\ref{sec:data_galah} describes the used data products of GALAH DR4 \citep{Buder2025b}, that is, observed spectra and their uncertainties, best-fit spectra, best-fit stellar labels of global stellar parameters and elemental abundances, extinction estimates, and additional quality information.
Section~\ref{sec:data_representative} explains how we select the variety of samples that form the starting point for our residual audit.

\subsection{GALAH DR4 spectra and labels}
\label{sec:data_galah}

We use data products from GALAH DR4 \citep{Buder2025b}, based on spectra observed with the HERMES spectrograph \citep{Barden2010, Brzeski2011, Heijmans2012, Farrell2014, Sheinis2015} on the Anglo-Australian Telescope as part of the GALAH survey \citep{DeSilva2015}. HERMES records high-resolution optical spectra with a nominal resolution of 28000 in four non-contiguous wavelength channels, covering the blue ($4713$--$4903\,\text{\AA}$), green ($5648$--$5873\,\text{\AA}$), red ($6478$--$6737\,\text{\AA}$), and near-infrared ($7585$--$7887\,\text{\AA}$) arms for up to 400 fibres of the 2dF position system \citep{Lewis2002, Miszalski2006}. GALAH DR4 combines observations from the main GALAH survey with K2-HERMES, TESS-HERMES, cluster, halo, and other targeted HERMES programs. We return to the specific target selection of the representative sample in Section~\ref{sec:data_representative}.

The reduced one-dimensional spectra are produced with the HERMES reduction pipeline of \citet{Kos2017}, updated for GALAH DR4. This pipeline performs the extraction, wavelength calibration, sky subtraction, telluric correction, barycentric correction, uncertainty estimation, and line-spread-function measurement for each spectrum. For each spectrum, GALAH DR4 provides the observed flux, the associated flux uncertainty, and the best-fitting synthetic spectrum inferred by the DR4 analysis and used to normalise the observation, as exemplified in Fig.~\ref{fig:example_spectrum_170710003201361}, where we visualise residuals in three different intensities based on their significance with respect to the reported flux uncertainty.

In using these products, we retain the GALAH DR4 masking of the strongly telluric-contaminated region $7585$--$7680\,\text{\AA}$. A reliable treatment of this wavelength range requires dedicated telluric modelling alongside the stellar model, as demonstrated by \citet{Saydjari2023} and \citet{Casey2026}, whereas our aim is to assess residual structure in the DR4 analysis products rather than to introduce an additional telluric-modelling step.

The GALAH DR4 stellar labels were inferred from a hybrid spectrum-synthesis workflow. The synthetic spectra were computed with \textsc{Spectroscopy Made Easy} \citep[SME;][]{Valenti1996, Piskunov2017} and one-dimensional MARCS model atmospheres \citep{Gustafsson2008}, using the GALAH line list developed from the compilation of \citet{Heiter2021}. To make the analysis feasible across the full GALAH wavelength range, GALAH DR4 trains neural-network interpolators on synthetic spectra in local regions of $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, and $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$ parameter space. These interpolators enable the simultaneous optimisation of global stellar parameters and elemental abundances across most of the observed wavelength range, including broad molecular C$_2$ and CN absorption features. The optimisation was done using up to 94\% of the pixels, with several pixels being excluded by default after initial tests indicated overall bad fit qualities for them \citep[for more details see Sec.~4.3.2 of][]{Buder2025b}. The 6\% of spectra that were synthesised but not used for optimisation are particularly interesting for this work and indicated as grey shaded regions in Fig.~\ref{fig:example_spectrum_170710003201361}.

The synthetic spectra include one-dimensional NLTE line formation for atomic lines where available. In GALAH DR4, this treatment is implemented through departure-coefficient grids from the BALDER/Multi3D framework and associated model atoms for H, Li, C, N, O, Na, Mg, Al, Si, K, Ca, Mn, Fe, and Ba \citep[e.g.][]{Amarsi2020b, Amarsi2022}. Other atomic transitions and molecular features are treated in one-dimensional LTE. This distinction is important for the interpretation of residuals: although GALAH DR4 models the broad C$_2$ and CN absorption that affected earlier analyses, these molecular features do not yet have the same NLTE treatment as the listed atomic transitions.

We use the GALAH DR4 labels as reference quantities against which residual structure can be tested. The main fitted stellar labels include the global stellar parameters $T_\mathrm{eff}$, $\log g$, $[\mathrm{Fe/H}]$, microturbulence velocity $v_\mathrm{mic}$, projected rotational velocity $v\sin i$, and radial velocity $v_\mathrm{rad}$. We also use the reported elemental abundances for Li, C, N, O, Na, Mg, Al, Si, K, Ca, Sc, Ti, V, Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Zn, Rb, Sr, Y, Zr, Mo, Ru, Ba, La, Ce, Nd, Sm, and Eu. These are reported as $[\mathrm{X/Fe}]$ for most elements, with lithium reported as $A(\mathrm{Li})$. We use the associated abundance flags to distinguish reliable detections from upper limits, non-detections, and measurements affected by optimisation or boundary issues.

GALAH DR4 reports spectra on their native pixel grids, with individual wavelength solutions for each observed spectrum. Because the data-driven models used in this work require all spectra to be represented on a shared wavelength grid, we interpolate the observed fluxes, best-fitting synthetic fluxes, and flux uncertainties onto a common wavelength grid for each HERMES arm. The construction of the common grid for the representative sample is described in Section~\ref{sec:data_representative}.

We also make use of auxiliary quantities reported by GALAH DR4. These include extinction-related quantities such as $E(B-V)$ from the dust maps of \citet{Schlegel1998} and $A_\mathrm{K_S}$ estimated via the Rayleigh-Jeans Color Excess method \citep{Majewski2011}, as reported by \citet{Buder2025b}. These quantities are not stellar-atmosphere labels fitted by the spectral synthesis, but they provide useful reference labels for testing whether residual spectra contain information associated with line-of-sight dust and interstellar absorption. GALAH DR4 also reports Gaussian fits to residuals at the expected positions of interstellar K\,I and three diffuse interstellar bands \citep[see Sec.~6.1.3][]{Buder2025b}, as well as measurements of emission signals in the Balmer lines \citep[see Sec.~6.1.4][]{Buder2025b}. These are diagnostic products of the residual spectra and are not included in the stellar synthetic spectra used in the DR4 analysis.

\subsection{Sample Selection}
\label{sec:data_representative}

% This paper is a first systematic audit, not yet a full survey-wide residual decomposition.
% Representative GALAH nights provides a compact, computationally manageable training set.
% There are other ways to create samples to probe residuals (e.g. from crossmatches with specific literature sources whose parameters such as logR'HK or X-ray luminosity one could probe for), but we will use one of the simplest approaches.

For this pilot study, we select spectra observed during three consecutive GALAH survey nights between 10 and 12 July 2017. This choice provides a compact but representative test set of ordinary GALAH targets, rather than a sample selected directly by stellar parameters, abundance quality, or residual behaviour. The selected fields are restricted to 22 pointings of nominal GALAH fields, where stars are selected randomly within a magnitude range (3 bright fields with $9 < V~/~\mathrm{mag} < 12$, 19 main fields with $12 < V~/~\mathrm{mag} < 12$, and one faint field with $9 < V~/~\mathrm{mag} < 14.3$) and therefore provide a useful first data set for testing whether systematic residual structure can be recovered across the typical stellar-parameter space of the survey. Across the three nights, 7627 observations of unique stars have been taken without repeated pointing at the same star.

In the following subsections, we describe how we create three separate samples as input for our residual analysis: a \textit{baseline} sample (Sec.~\ref{sec:sample_baseline}) with some quality cuts, an \textit{abundance} sample with additional requirements for quality and abundance measurements (Sec.~\ref{sec:sample_abundance}), and finally an \textit{activity} sample for which we select only sources with available activity indicators from a literature source (Sec.~\ref{sec:sample_activity}).

\subsubsection{Baseline Sample}
\label{sec:sample_baseline}

We filter these observations with several quality criteria. For each, the DR4 pipeline needs to have converged to a fit solution (\texttt{flag\_sp} $<2048$) with reasonable fit-quality of $\chi^2_\mathrm{sp} < 2$. This cut primarily removes spectra with strong molecular features and poor global fits, especially the coolest giants.
We further require finite values of both $E(B-V)$ and $A_\mathrm{K_S}$ and exposures in all four CCDs (\texttt{snr\_px\_ccd} > 10).
We do not remove spectra with emission-line signatures or binary flags, because such spectra provide relevant tests of whether non-stellar or poorly modelled spectral structure is recovered by the residual analysis.
These criteria leave a baseline sample of 7087 spectra. Each spectrum is from a unique star and no repeat observations are taken across the nights.

The distribution of this sample in the Kiel diagram is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:labels_m1_teff_logg_feh}. The sample covers the main stellar types observed by GALAH, spanning approximately $3000 < T_\mathrm{eff}/\mathrm{K} < 8000$, $0 < \log(g/\mathrm{cm\,s^{-2}}) < 5.5$, and $-1.5 < \mathrm{[Fe/H]} < 0.75$. \SB{Add coverage of $E(B-V)$ and $A_\mathrm{K_S}$ as well as the typical signal-to-noise distributions.}
This broad coverage allows us to test whether residual structure is associated with the main stellar labels, rather than only with a narrow region of parameter space.

For the representative sample, we construct a shared wavelength grid separately for each HERMES arm. We define each grid using the largest starting wavelength, the median wavelength dispersion, and the smallest final wavelength among the selected spectra. This gives common wavelength ranges of
\begin{align}
    4719.0299..&0.0460..4893.8808\,\text{\AA}, \notag \\
    5655.5298..&0.0547..5863.0182\,\text{\AA}, \notag \\
    6486.0104..&0.0631..6726.4572\,\text{\AA}\text{, and} \notag \\
    7689.1261..&0.0735..7874.3127\,\text{\AA} \notag
\end{align}
for the blue, green, red, and near-infrared arms, respectively. We interpolate the observed flux, best-fitting synthetic flux, and flux uncertainty onto these grids before constructing the residual matrix.

We define the residual spectrum as the difference between the observed and best-fitting synthetic flux,
\begin{equation}
    \Delta f_\lambda = f_{\lambda,\mathrm{obs}} - f_{\lambda,\mathrm{model}}, \label{eq:def_residuals}
\end{equation}
and use the reported flux uncertainties $\sigma_{f,\lambda}$ to calculate a residual significance,
\begin{equation}
    s_\lambda = \frac{\vert \Delta f_\lambda \vert}{\sigma_{f,\lambda}}, \label{eq:def_significance}
\end{equation}
and to construct inverse-variance weights,
\begin{equation}
    w_\lambda = \sigma_{f,\lambda}^{-2}. \label{eq:def_ivar}
\end{equation}
After requiring finite interpolated fluxes and uncertainties across the common wavelength grid, the final matrix contains 5314 spectra and 13927 wavelength pixels. This residual matrix forms the input to the data-driven models analysed in the following sections.

\subsubsection{Abundance Sample}
\label{sec:sample_abundance}

Based on chemical evolution studies \citep[e.g.][]{Kobayashi2020}, we know that the chemical enhancement of several elements is strongly correlated due to their similar nucleosynthetic origins. As a result, the absorption lines of these elements are also expected to be strongly correlated. We will use this insight and representative elements of a range of nucleosynthesis or element groups to search for residual lines who are correlated with these enrichment patterns. In particular, we use Mg to represent core-collapse supernovae-dominated elements, Mn to represent supernovae Ia-dominated elements, Na to represent the behaviour of several odd-Z elements, and Ba to represent asymptotic giant branch star-dominated neutron-capture elements. We only select spectra from the \textit{baseline} sample which also fulfil $\texttt{flag\_x\_fe} = 0$ for $\texttt{x} \in \mathrm{[Na, Mg, Mn, Ba]}$. As we are searching for possibly smaller signals, our changes are higher, if we restrict ourselves to stars with typically narrow absorption lines, where most of the absorption is focussed in one or at least few pixels of our GALAH spectra. We thus further restrict this sample to slow-rotating stars ($\texttt{vsini} < 15\,\mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}$) and also cut a few dozen stars (less than 1\%) when enforcing $T_\mathrm{eff} < 6500\,\mathrm{K}$. We show the destribution of stellar parameters for the resulting \textit{activity} sample of 3826 spectra Fig.~\ref{fig:labels_m2_teff_logg_feh_xfe}. For further information, we also show the neglected hot stars and fast rotators as grey crosses and an inset of one of the abundance distributions, [Fe/H] vs. [Mg/Fe].

Ideally, we would want to also include the r-process enrichment pattern. However, Eu, as the sole element in the GALAH range known to be strongly dominated by this enrichment patter, is not well modelled in GALAH DR4. Similary, Sm, as the next most dominated element is not detected often enough. And Nd, as the third most dominated element is already showing a very similar pattern to the remaining s-process elements, and would decrease the useful training set from 3826 to 2663 spectra (a decrease of 30 percent). We hence do not attempt to search for correlations of spectrum residuals with this pattern.

\subsubsection{Activity Sample}
\label{sec:sample_activity}

With a third \textit{activity} sample, we aim to explore how well we can predict stellar activity. \subsection{How significant are the residuals and where?}
\label{sec:analysis_significance}

Two things are important to consider: How significant is a residual with respect to the underlying noise? What is the sign of a significant residual?

If the observed model has a stronger absorption than what is modelled ($\Delta f_\lambda < 0$), the model may lack absorption opacity, underestimate the strength of a blend, omit a relevant line, or misrepresent the line-formation physics. If the observed flux is higher than the synthesis ($\Delta f_\lambda > 0$), the model may over-predict absorption, miss chromospheric line-core filling, or absorb continuum-normalisation and reduction systematics into the fit. A signed residual analysis therefore separates under-predicted and over-predicted spectral structure, rather than treating all mismatch as a single unsigned noise term.

So we will quantify at the following with our baseline sample:
\begin{itemize}
    \item What is the distribution of residuals $\Delta f_\lambda$ across the wavelength range, both median residuals and 16th-84th percentiles? For each pixel, we calculate the 16/50/84th percentiles of residual and plot it across the 7087 spectra (Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics} top).
    \item How significant are these residuals typically across the wavelength range? For each pixel, we calculate the 16/50/84th percentiles of the significance $s_{\lambda}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics} middle).
    \item How often are residuals significant for a given pixel? For each pixel, we calculate the percentage of residuals above/below 1, 2, and $3\sigma$ significance. We show this distribution in Fig.~\ref{fig:residual_statistics} (bottom) with blue regions showing how often the observation are more than $2\sigma$ shallower and red regions showing how often the observations are more than $2\sigma$ deeper (We attach the same panels for $1\sigma$ and $3\sigma$ in Fig.~\ref{fig:percentage_residuals_1_and_2_sigma}).
    \item To get a quantitative feeling, for how often residuals are significant (and how large their are for typical GALAH observations), we show cumulative distribution functions of the significance of residuals (both median as well as 16th and 84th percentile) in Fig.~\ref{fig:cdf_significance_residual_flux_percentiles_m1}, with an inset of the cumulative distribution of absolute residuals.
\end{itemize}

A first motivation is "What if we could model 100% of a spectrum?" While this is a reference to a meme, it opens up a specific avenue of investigation: What effects are currently preventing us from using 100% of the information that observations give us (that is, flux and flux uncertainty as a function of wavelength)? In particular, based on my analysis of GALAH DR4, where I have traced the percentage of pixels used for the synthesis, only up to 94% of the pixels can be used for optimisation (others I had to exclude because of usual bad fits as described out in \citet[][Sec.~4.3.2]{Buder2025b}).

Being able to fit spectra well is important for a lot of domains: it is timely for Gaia DR4, which will deliver time-domain spectroscopy for millions of stars. It is important for planet hunters (or hunters of any non-stellar signal that is potentially dwarfed by stellar effects). Similarly we have not identified all lines in spectra and we cannot model everything realistic (e.g. chromosphere or other effects influencing Balmer lines -> Amarsi) > Galah DR4 only able to include only up to 94%! What if some signatures (e.g. of rare elements) hidden? Only recently extracted C and N abundances from GALAH spectra based on proof-of-concept from CEMP stars (Cotar) -> aim to use Cannon to see which nucleosynthesis group residuals may correlate with. And of course extinction! -> exciting potential to let spectra tell us about this effect that significantly effects photometry (important beyond Gaiasphere with precise enough parallax measurements). Not to forgot potential to predict 3D non-LTE effects (or even corrections?) Potential: train Cannon (or any spectrum interpolator) to map between cheap 1D LTE and 3D NLTE instead of having to calculate more expensive 1D NLTE or even more expensive 3D NLTE models for each spectrum (it will still require the training set to be done in 3D NLTE, but then the 1D LTE -> 3D NLTE mapping could approached as a data-driven mapping rather than an astrophysical numerical one)!

Here are a few ideas (already pointing into a direction that I believe we could use a data-driven approach to tell us which parts of a spectrum are susceptible to these influences within the GALAH wavelength range):
- 3D NLTE effects, e.g. \citep[][]{Amarsi2018} http://adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2018A%26A...615A.139A (currently we are only modelling 1D LTE or 1D NLTE at best) -> all lines, but particularly the two Balmer lines of Halpha and Hbeta
- missing line data (e.g. a specific line is not in the line list of GALAH which is heavily based of the Gaia ESO linelist by \citet{Heiter2021} https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2021A&A...645A.106H and thus is not in the model) -> this is possibly the case for ~20 lines 
- lines that are strongly affected by stellar activity (while the CaHK lines are not in the GALAH wavelength range, \citet{Spina2020} https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2020ApJ...895...52S showed for example a strong change in lines such as the Ba II line at 5853Å and how stellar activity significantly effects measurements above log R'HK of -4.6, but even already starting from -5.0. I am able to create a training set 2 of 228 GALAH dwarf stars and spectra that has log R'HK information from \citet{Zhang2024} https://ui.adsabs.harvard.edu/abs/2024A&A...688A..23Z to probe which lines are most sensitive to this label.
